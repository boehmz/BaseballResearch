                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Musgrove has a one-seam sinker that has the drop of a sinker and the horizontal movement of a cutter! That's weird. He also has decent command and a really good sideways slider that reminds one a bit of Corey Kluber's breaking ball. His change and curve are okay. There's a lot to like here, even if it didn't quite come together in a package that convinces projections that he'll be much more than an average pitcher next year. One thing that was interesting was that he nibbled a bit more in his last five games than he did in his first five, and that led to fewer homers. If he can put together the decent strikeout rate with the plus walk rate and also know when to leave the zone to avoid 'giving in,' Musgrove could do better than 1.25 homers per nine, which would would make him a high-threes ERA guy with league-average strikeouts and a tiny walk rate. That'll be useful in most leagues! At his velocity, however, it's not likely that he turns into an ace without a healthy dollop of luck. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>There are some unique things about the movement of Musgrove's pitches -- his slider is super horizontal, and his fastballs very vertical, including a one-seamer -- but it's unclear that uniqueness alone can fuel stardom. Still, in the second half of his debut, Musgrove showed signs of being able to adjust to the league and put everything in its right place. He could still be a real-life two or three.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
