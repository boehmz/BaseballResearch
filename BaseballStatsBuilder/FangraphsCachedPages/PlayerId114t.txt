As with extreme ground-ball pitchers, extreme fly ball pitchers can defy their FIPs (since 2004, <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/leaders.aspx?pos=all&stats=pit&lg=all&qual=y&type=8&season=2013&month=0&season1=2004&ind=0&team=0&rost=0&age=0&filter=10863&players=0">this group of fly ball pitchers</a> beat their FIPs by 20 points). The main obstacle to his success? That very faucet of pitching talent. The Rays rotation, still containing <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/statss.aspx?playerid=3184&position=P">David Price</a> at the time of publishing, may not have 180 innings to give to Odorizzi. But at age 24, Jake will no doubt find his fair share of innings -- even if David Price is still in St. Petersburg -- via injury or ineffectiveness. That might have already happened with the announcement that <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/statss.aspx?playerid=4371&position=P">Jeremy Hellickson</a> will miss a large chunk of the season after elbow surgery. It would not be unfair to expect a better-than-average ERA from Odorizzi, but his strikeout rate will never be elite -- his stuff just isn't elite -- and his FIP might never match his ERA. (<a href="https://twitter.com/BradleyWoodrum">@BradleyWoodrum</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>There's a chance he spends another season shuttling between the majors and minors, but Odorizzi ranks as arguably the Rays best pitching prospect. Odorizzi could already be in line for 150+ innings and possibly a 3.80ish ERA.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After much of his prospect shine wore off in the upper levels of the Royals' farm system, Jake Odorizzi established himself as a legit big league starter with a little help from Tampa Bay Rays magic. The righty featured his new split-change much more in 2014, which helped him improve against right-handed batters. He is as extreme a fly ball pitcher as exists in the game right now, which isn't surprising for a pitcher who works up in the zone with a fastball that isn't exactly awe-inspiring. The home runs will come, but, with some improvements in his command, Odorizzi can work up and down and use spacious Tropicana Field to his advantage. Entering his age-25 season, Odorizzi looks like a pitcher learning as he goes, tweaking his pitch selection to yield better results and work best with what he has. Shortening up his slider and using the split-change can only help his fastball and curveball, and that means good things for his numbers on the whole. Until the league shows they can take advantage of his propensity to work the top of the strike zone, let Odorizzi's improvements speak for themselves through his ERA. 2015 is his chance to put it all together and put that process into action. (Drew Fairservice)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>With an unconventional desire to work up in the zone and an ever-changing pitch selection, Jake Odorizzi is a tough one to pin down. There is lots to like about his 2014 and his 2015 looks bright indeed.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>With a riding fastball that he can locate up in the zone, and a nice split-finger changeup that drops five inches off of that trajectory, Odorizzi has two-thirds of a strong starter's arsenal. The problem has been finding that third pitch. This last season, he debuted a new breaking ball of sorts, a hybrid cutter/slider/slutter thing that was supposed to be what he needed against right-handers in particular -- they have enjoyed facing the Rays' righty over the years, especially when it comes to home runs. Turns out, the cutter was worse than the slider when it came to home runs allowed and whiffs garnered. The slight edge the cutter had over his previous slider came in ground balls, but that rate (40%) was still not impressive. If he never finds that breaking pitch, we may look back on last year as the 26-year-old's best. In the meantime, though, while his velocity is average and his home park is in Tampa, he's a fairly safe mid-round bet with a tiny bit of strikeout upside if he refines a breaker. In dynasty leagues, though, it may be time to shop him around. Other than perhaps a knuckle curve, there aren't really any more breaking balls to try. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Jake Odorizzi was once a kid with gas and great slider. Now he's got average velocity, a good splitter, and is searching for a useable breaking ball. Pitching is weird, but this righty probably can't be an upper-tier pitcher without that third pitch, so listen up when he talks about adding a new wrinkle this spring. It might matter.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Odorizzi works vertically in the strike zone while painting corners, and like many Rays pitchers in 2016, he was victimized by the long ball at a high rate due to the fact he works up in the zone so frequently with his fastball.
