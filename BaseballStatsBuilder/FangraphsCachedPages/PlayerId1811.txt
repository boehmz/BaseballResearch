He was as much as anybody could have asked for in the role, with a 4.08 ERA and even better peripherals, recording a 3.0 strikeout-to-walk ratio. It's difficult to imagine him maintaining such success given a minor league track record of sub-3.0 K/BBs, but he doesn't need to in order to succeed in the swingman role in store for him in Milwaukee.                 <tr id="so5" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Of starting pitchers who threw at least 100 innings last season, Estrada owned the eighth-highest strikeout rate in the league (9.30 K/9). It appears sustainable, as he has posted a swinging-strike rate above 10.0% over the last three years in Milwaukee. The 29-year-old also limits the free passes, which lowers his WHIP. His 1.14 WHIP was the same as the ones sported by CC Sabathia and Chris Sale -- not to mention his strikeout rate was higher -- yet he will be available several rounds later. The only question marks surrounding Estrada are his home runs (1.17 HR/9) and the fact that he’s never thrown more than 138.1 innings in a single major-league season. The Brewers’ rotation projects to be filled with young, unpredictable starters, and Estrada should receive the opportunity to start for the entire year. That should result in sneaky value for fantasy owners. (JP Breen)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Estrada is an underrated fantasy starter who is a good bet to provide above-average production in strikeouts and WHIP in the middle rounds of your next fantasy draft.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>It proved to be a tale of two halves for Marco Estrada. The right-hander dished up home runs in the first half like Subway does obnoxious commercials, allowing 1.82 home runs per nine innings, which resulted in a bloated ERA that made him unownable in all leagues. The core skills remained, though. He continued to miss bats and limit the free passes; thus, when the home run rate normalized in the second half, he became nasty. His 2.15 post-All-Star ERA benefited from a .199 batting average on balls in play, but again, the core skills illustrate his true potential. He owned the seventh-best swinging-strike rate (11.8%) and sixth-best strikeout-to-walk ratio (5.09 K/BB) in the second half. Fantasy owners should be worried his velocity dropped yet again, but he continued to miss bats because he relies on sequencing and a plus change-up. Injury concerns also exist. Considering his highest SIERA the past three seasons was 3.51 and he can strike out almost a batter per inning, owners would be wise to have Estrada on their sleeper list this spring. (JP Breen)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Estrada is the type of pitcher who helps you win fantasy championships. His brutal first half masked his true abilities; he can provide above-average strikeouts, along with a solid WHIP and ERA, and that's not often available in the mid-to-late rounds.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Estrada has a lot in common with a solid third starter. He has struck out 8.5 batters and walked just 2.4 batters per nine in his major league career. But every year that he’s made at least two starts, he’s allowed more than 10 percent of his flyballs to leave the park. It may be the result of his reliance on deception; his average fastball is slower than 90 mph. Whatever the reason, after 541 career innings, it’s difficult to expect that rate to regress. Estrada did get traded away from homer-friendly Miller Park this offseason, but he landed in another hitter-friendly park in Toronto. Estrada’s likeliest path to fantasy value at this point is to land in the bullpen, which does not have an established closer. Still, Estrada is an AL-only option at best this season. (Scott Spratt)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Estrada’s trade from Milwaukee to Toronto will likely do little for his homer-prone ways. As such, it’s difficult to see him having fantasy value unless he lands in an inexperienced bullpen and can ride the strikeout to high-leverage innings.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>When you glance from ERA (3.13) to FIP (4.40) in 2015, Estrada goes from "ace" to, well, "not ace." Estrada posted a career best ERA, but also a career low in strikeout rate and a career high in walk rate, which is not something you want to see. Estrada got by on a .216 batting average on balls in play and a career best 9% home run to fly ball rate. The BABIP isn't exactly sustainable, but I'd easily bet on something around his career mark of .261, maybe lower. Estrada's 21% soft contact rate is excellent and his 52% flyball rate is among the highest in the game. Softly hit balls have a low BABIP, fly balls have a low BABIP, ergo Estrada has a low BABIP. The problem is fly balls also turn into homers, and Estrada had some luck there. His home run to fly ball rate was good, but he was only average at avoiding hard contact. Basically, Estrada is a guy with a middling ability to avoid walks, who gives up a lot of fly balls and a lot of soft contact -- but not a particularly low amount of hard contact. I expect the BABIP and home run to fly ball rate to rise. I'm no pitching expert but more base runners and more homers does not sound like a recipe for success. He'll continue to beat his FIP and likely post an ERA in the 3.90-4.00 range, but the floor is low and without any strikeouts, he doesn't offer much upside. (Chad Young)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>How do you feel about pitchers with 13 wins and 3.13 ERAs? But what about guys with few strikeouts and lots of home runs allowed? Estrada is more the latter than the former -- if you can sell that ERA, you should, but I'll be staying far, far away.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

It's 2017 - fastball velocity is at an all time high, and all hitters try to do is "elevate and celebrate". Yet here we are, staring at Estrada's 88 mph fastball, and 8.5 K/9. Sometimes, the outliers are more exciting than the trends. The past two seasons have been great for Estrada. When he's not battling through a back injury, he pitches deep into games, gets average to above average strike outs, and controls his walks. When Estrada is on, he is a threat to pitch 8 innings, with 8 strike outs, and less than 2 walks. When he loses his pin point control... the fans in the outfield better bring their gloves. The growing trend for hitters to try and hit the ball in the air plays both into Estrada's strategy, but also exposes him to his biggest flaw - giving up home runs. Expect the 2017 iteration of Marco Estrada to once again provide innings, wins, average strikeouts, and low walk rates. If the aging curve bites Estrada hard, he doesn't have a lot of room for error, and things could get ugly in a hurry. (Mike Sonne)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Marco Estrada has been one of the best pitchers on the Toronto Blue Jays.
