                <tr id="so3" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>An undersized right-hander who gets by with fine command and a deep mix of pitches, Tomlin didn't sniff prospect lists coming into 2010 but finished the year taking turns in Cleveland's ho-hum rotation. The former 19th-round pick in the 2006 draft doesn't blow anyone away with his stuff -- he'll crack 90 MPH with his fastball on a windy day -- but he also features a mid-80s cutter, a low-80s changeup and a mid-70s curveball. In Triple-A, he got plenty of lucky bounces in posting a 2.68 ERA that belied mediocre peripheral stats (6.7 K/9, 2.8 BB/9 and a 4.02 FIP). With the Tribe, Tomlin whiffed just 5.3 batters per nine frames, walked 2.3 per nine and had a 4.59 FIP in 73 innings. Using his modest fastball less than 50% of the time, he got plenty of swings on pitches out of the zone (33.1%) but opponents hit the ball skyward. Tomlin's 28.4% ground-ball rate was second-lowest among starters pitching at least 70 innings. His GB rate was 36% at Double-A in 2009 and 40 at Triple-A in 2010. Progressive Field isn't a homer-happy venue, but Tomlin has little margin for error if he's not getting many Ks and he's tempting fate with deep drives to the warning track.                 <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>There are two fundamental Josh Tomlin truths and two fundamental Josh Tomlin questions. The truths -- he will walk almost no one (elite 3% walk rate over the last two years) and he will give up a lot of fly balls (not-so-elite 39% fly ball rate). The big questions are can he strike people out and can he keep those fly balls in the yard? Tomlin's 22% strikeout rate the last two years was good enough to give him the 21st best strikeout minus walk rate, while 2010-2012 strikeout was just 13%. In those early years, though, his home run per fly ball rate was just 11% versus 15% the last two years (fourth highest in baseball). Check out the pieces that are there though. In 2015, one pitcher had a 20-22% K-rate, with a 12-14% home run to fly ball rate without posting an elite ground ball rate. James Shields gave up fewer fly balls than Tomlin, but also walked nearly twice as many players, and posted a 3.64 SIERA. Tomlin may not have a rotation spot on Opening Day, and he won't get drafted in any but the deepest leagues, but watch closely -- there is real breakout potential here, even at age 31. (Chad Young)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Josh Tomlin has never had a fantasy-worth season, but he has shown the components (decent strikeout rate, elite walk rate, high fly ball rate, acceptable home run to flyball rate) of a successful pitcher. He's not a draft candidate, but if he gets a shot in the rotation, watch those peripherals and don't be afraid to take a shot on him.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Given how few hitters he walks, he's a Minnesota Twin through and through. I don't care what the name on the front of his uniform says, give him a snow blower and a Menards BIG Card and send him on his way. Tomlin continued his stinginess last year, walking just 2.8% of batters, the lowest among all qualified pitchers. On the other hand, his 16.3% strikeout rate ranked 68th out of 73 pitchers and dropped by over five percent from 2014-15, driven by a significant decrease in swinging-strike rate. While Tomlin traded in some four-seamers for cutters, he saw the starkest drop among his secondary pitches and particularly his curve. After being a significantly above-average pitch early in his career, Tomlin's curve fell to be one of the worst in all of baseball in 2016. The good news is that Tomlin improved in terms of his ground ball rate, which reached a career high 44% in 2016. The increase was buoyed in large part by a significant rise in four-seam grounders. Between the new worm-burning potential of his fastball and strong ground ball rates on his other pitches, Tomlin may have reinvented himself. But he's still a Twin in my book.
