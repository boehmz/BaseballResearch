Now he finds himself as the de facto No. 2 starter on the Mariners for 2011. He is going to receive a long rope, which is beneficial when you make a choice for a starting pitcher, but, unfortunately, Vargas doesn’t offer much for the distinguishing fantasy shopper. Jason Vargas on the Mariners is worth remembering only to serve as an occasional single-serving free-agent pick-up when he gets to face a tepid offense at home.                 <tr id="so4" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Jason Vargas has value as a starter, but his tendency to give up long balls limits his value in games away from Seattle. Vargas' 2011 stats look okay with a 4.25 ERA, 1.31 WHIP, 131 strikeouts and 10 wins. The problem is that he is two different pitchers on the road and at home. On the road in his career he is 8-21 with an 5.41 ERA, a 1.3 home runs per nine and 1.6 strikeout-to-walk ratio. Now at home, in Seattle, he is 16-15 with a 3.63 ERA , a 0.9 HR/9 and a K/BB rate of 2.4. He is also a decent bet to use at other home-run-suppressing parks. Vargas' overall skills (strikeout and walk rates, and fastball speed) are relatively constant, so there are no signs of his skills degrading. Don't draft him expecting to use him in each start -- use him for starts at home and sit him on the road. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Vargas is only a platoon candidate and should also only be started at home because of his home run tendencies.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

In 2013, he did produce a noteworthy increase in strikeout rate, which may have been caused by his rather dramatic shift in pitch selection (towards more curveballs and four-seam fastballs). If he can maintain his 2013 pitching mix and K%, a ranking closer to #80 might be in order, but none of his pitches other than the changeup get average whiffs or ground balls for their pitch type. Four years and $32 million was not out of line with his free agent value, and if Vargas was far from great, he projected to be roughly league average, give the Royals some stabilizing innings, and, best of all, was a fly ball pitcher who would be pitching in a favorable park with a great defensive outfield. Vargas was his usual soft-tossing lefty self in 2014 and the first part of 2015 for Kansas City, not generating too many strikeouts (his high-80s fastball is eminently hittable), but keeping his walks down and the ball in the park. His peripherals were all down in 2015, and though he kept his ERA under four, it was a portent: he pitched only nine games before going down and having Tommy John surgery. When healthy, Vargas has real world value as a mid- to back-of-the-rotation starter. In fantasy, his value is pretty much restricted to deeper AL-only leagues when healthy. There is a chance he could pitch in 2016, depending on how his recovery goes, but there is no need to spend a draft pick on him, though. It is better to wait and see as there will be plenty of other similar options available in the meantime. (Matt Klaassen)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Jason Vargas is a useful starter in the real world, but in fantasy his value is limited to mostly to deeper AL-only leagues, and that is when he is healthy.
