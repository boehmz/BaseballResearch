                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Drew Pomeranz will finally get his shot this year. The now 26-year-old southpaw demonstrated an ability to get major league hitters out last season, albeit in just 10 starts and an additional 10 relief appearances. Even in the 69 inning sample size, Pomeranz was able to induce a fair share of strikeouts and swinging strikes. Watch his walk rate as his 51.8% first strike rate — as both a starter and reliever — was a far cry from the 61.1% F-strike% the league average starter posted in 2014. Pomeranz may be prone to the occasional burst of gopher-itis but his home park should help keep his home run rate close to league average. Overall Pomeranz is probably best fits as a stream starter as opposite handed hitters could give him fits. His knuckle curve works as a great secondary pitch, but until he utilizes his change-up to help neutralize right-handed hitters, Pomeranz may find himself dancing through raindrops too often to be a strong fantasy pitcher.<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Although this is the first season Pomeranz figures to break camp with a major league team, don't be too optimistic on his production just yet. He has no issues against lefties, but right-handed hitters could be his undoing as a starter, in both real life and fantasy. </td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After posting a rather sketchy 4.63 ERA and 4.19 FIP as a starter, the A's moved Pomeranz to the bullpen in May and (outside of a spot start in July), the lefty didn't look back and flourished out of the pen. Pom saw his strikeout rate tick up, his walks tick down, and he displayed a 3.01 FIP in support of a 2.61 ERA. Right-handed hitters seem likely to give him fits so perhaps a bullpen role would be the wisest use of his skill set. Coming out as a lefty specialist seems like the worst case scenario, with him becoming a seventh or eighth inning guy also an acceptable role. The southpaw is an okay albeit uninspiring play in standard strikeout leagues, but his walk rate, even in relief where it was lower than when he started, was a meh 3.04 per nine last season. If the Friars place him in the pen, expect some holds and strikeouts with a WHIP you can stomach, but not love. He'd be someone to think about in the final round or two in holds leagues. Conversely, Pom is worth a late round pick as a starter as well, though a lot of his shiny ERAs have been products a great batting averages on balls in play. In 2014 he owned a .244 BABIP and last year Pomeranz posted a .270 mark. His fly ball tendencies help keep those figures low, and moving to Petco won't hurt him in that department, however even with his strikeout upside, his walk rate and struggles against right-handers make him a late round target regardless of his role. (David Wiers)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Pomeranz, the starter-turned-reliever, may once again will find himself competing for a spot in a starting rotation come spring training, this time as a member of the Padres. Of course, the 28-year-old Red Sox has had fewer chances to be healthy than the 37-year-old Dodger, and maybe his stuff hasn't produced exactly the same standout whiff and strikeout rates -- perhaps because his command is a bit worse, and his curve doesn't quite have the same spin-rate excellence. That means the ceiling on Pomeranz is lower, but his floor might be higher, too. Can the previously injury-riddled lefty stay healthy, especially now that he's throwing the curve 40% of the time? We don't really have the same attitudes about high-volume curveball usage that we used to, but the track record suggests we should be nervous. Still, project Pomeranz for 120-140 innings, pay for that projection, and you'll have a little bit of upside baked into your price. He could stay healthy all year, or maintain the low batting average on balls in play that he's had ever since he started throwing the curve so often. Either thing would give you more than you paid for. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Over the last three years, batters are hitting under .220 combined on the curve and fastball from Pomeranz.
