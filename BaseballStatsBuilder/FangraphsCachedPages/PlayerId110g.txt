                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Look at Gerrit Cole's year-end stats and you might not get it. He didn't strike out batters at better than a league-average rate, his ground-ball rate was good-not-great, and his good stats were seemingly built on the back of excellent control. Watch the game, however, and you know that there's more on the way. His excellent command of two mid-90s fastballs is the basis of his excellence, but he's starting to freelance beyond with his secondary stuff. His curve is excellent in getting both whiffs and ground-balls. His slider is good for whiffs. His changeup is only about league-average when it comes to peripherals, but it plays up against lefties. Increased use of those pitches led to a jump in his strikeout rate in the second half. Those numbers -- he struck out a quarter of the guys he saw in the second half while keeping his excellent control -- are the basis of calling Cole a fantasy ace in the coming year. With no threat of an innings cap, youth on his side, devastating stuff, a strikeout per inning, excellent control, smart defense behind him, and a homer-suppressing park, Cole checks all the boxes. He's one of the best young pitchers to take a chance on after you've taken three or four offensive studs.  (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/">Eno Sarris</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>You never know what will happen as the spring progresses and pundits pick their poison, but talk could be about Michael Wacha and Jose Fernandez as the young pitchers closest to fantasy acedom this season. But Gerrit Cole has just as much of a chance -- maybe better -- to end the season as a top-12 fantasy pitcher.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Cole is on the verge of being one of the elite pitchers in the game. He already displays three of the four traits you'd like to see from an ace. He has been striking out batters at decent clip (a strikeout per inning), walking them at a below average clip (2.6 walks per nine) and keeping the ball on the ground (49% ground-ball rate). While these numbers are good, they are not great. He ended up with the 32nd-best FIP, and his 3.65 ERA was actually worse than league average. The fourth trait Cole wasn't able to display in 2014 was health. He went on the disabled list twice for a total of 70 days with shoulder issues. Nothing predicts future injuries like past injuries, so he may end up missing more time in the future. After coming back from the DL, he put up better numbers, with his strikeout minus walk rate going from 13% in the first half to 23% in the second half. For 2015, it is tough to figure out which pitcher will show up. Value him for something like 25 starts, 140 strikeouts with a 3.50 ERA. He could perform even better if he continues to throw like he did in the the second half of 2015. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Gerrit Cole enters 2015 with large number of possible outcomes because of injury issues which split an okay first half from his great second half in 2014.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

In fact, looking at peripheral plate discipline metrics shows he has been really consistent, and this author is inclined to think the underwhelming strikeout rate in 2013 was a result of a statistical noise in a still-small sample. Because his swing rates both in and out of the zone, his contact rates (likewise), his zone rate, his first-strike percentage -- they're all remarkably consistent. And if you extrapolate his win above replacement values from his first two partial seasons, you'll realize he has all along been the five WAR starter he was in 2015. Unfortunately, it's this author's job to nitpick (although it's not explicitly stated in the job description): Cole's dipped so beautifully below two walks per nine last year, but with little change to hitters' plate discipline peripherals, his 2016 walk rate will likely be closer to his career norm. And Steamer sees that, projecting a 6.1% walk rate in 2016 versus his 6.0% career rate. Regardless, Cole is a bona fide ace with bona fide heat. It'll be interesting to see how he uses his slider in 2016 -- he ramped up its usage in 2015 and generated excellent results. If he happens to make a big step forward in 2016, this author wagers it will be due to further refinement and added use of his secondary offerings. Cole might be just outside the top 10 starters, but don't wait much longer. It might be worth shelling out a little extra for his combination of effectiveness and consistency anyway. (Alex Chamberlain)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Gerrit Cole has been remarkably consistent through his first three seasons, making him not only a sure-fire top-tier starter but a reliable one at that.                 <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Cole's season mirrors Matt Harvey's in some ways. Coming off an ace-caliber campaign in 2015, Cole struggled through an injury-shortened season (116 innings). Where Cole's outcome differs is that he wasn't cut open as a result of his injuries. He had three DL stints, the last two both being for elbow inflammation, but he's been able to avoid Tommy John surgery to this point. Like Harvey, Cole's skills fell back, but because he is so good he was still average skill-wise with 12% K%-BB% rate. His velocity held relatively firm, too. There is still tons of appeal with a healthy Cole, but that is obvious to everyone with his price still relatively high as a top-30 starter. The interesting thing about Cole is that even his 2015 breakout kind of left us wanting more. Even with a 2.60 ERA, 1.09 WHIP, 24% strikeout rate, and 4.6 K:BB ratio in 208 innings, it just feels like he is capable of much more. That seems ridiculous to say, but the size and arsenal of the former number one overall pick yields massive expectations. If the elbow stays intact, Cole is one of a handful of pitchers going outside the top-25 who can be fantasy's best pitcher. (<a href="http://twitter.com/sporer">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>A trio of DL stints caused by a balky right elbow limited Cole to an underwhelming 116-inning season.
