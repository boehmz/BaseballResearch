                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>Before the 2008 season, Baltimore sent lefty Erik Bedard to the Mariners in exchange for what was commonly referred to as "George Sherrill, Adam Jones, and three minor leaguers." Tillman – one those minor leaguers – was ranked by Baseball America as Seattle's No. 3 prospect, but ended the 2007 season as a 19-year-old having pitched all of 103 innings at even the high Class A level. His performance in the meantime suggests that he might be remembered as the centerpiece of that deal. In 2008, Tillman posted a 3.41 FIP at Double-A Bowie on the strength of a 10.22 K/9. Likewise, he began 2009 well, posting a 2.76 FIP at Triple-A Norfolk with excellent peripherals: a 9.22 K/9 versus only a 2.42 BB/9. Upon his promotion to The Show in late July, Tillman wasn't quite so dominant but showed flashes of brilliance, including an eight-strikeout performance (in only 26 batters faced) versus the Yankees on Sept. 11.<br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>There are a number of explanations for Tillman's struggles through his first 65 major league innings. Most notable is the decrease in strikeouts. Tillman struck out only 5.4 per nine. This isn't unheard of: teammate David Hernandez, for example, experienced a similar thing after he was promoted. But while scouts have never viewed Hernandez's stuff with any awe, Tillman's been highly touted. Also of concern is Tillman's home-run rate of 2.08 per nine innings. That's due, in part, to an unlucky HR/FB rate, but also to the fact that Tillman is close to extreme in his fly-ball tendencies. Pitchers like Rich Harden can get away with it on account of high K rates, but, generally speaking, fly balls = home runs = unsightly ERAs. Finally, fatigue is a real possibility. Tillman pitched about 25% more innings in 2009 than 2008. In the final analysis, Tillman projects to be very good, but a lack of experience – not to mention frightening AL East lineups – might cause some growing pains in the immediate future. (Carson Cistulli)</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

In April, May and July, he had FIPs under 4.00, but in two August starts he struggled mightily, allowing eight runs in 9.2 innings. To make matters worse, his Triple-A numbers paled in comparison to his numbers in Baltimore -- a 6.25 FIP in the minors compared to 3.99 in Baltimore. As with many young pitchers, Tillman needs to work on his control and avoid home runs to find success. In 13 starts for Baltimore, he allowed 3.63 walks per nine and .73 home runs per nine. If he can keep his numbers at that point, or lower, he could continue to post a sub-4.00 FIP and may find an ERA to match. Unfortunately, without a serious uptick in strikeouts, his fantasy value will be limited. (Chad Young)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Tillman pitched decently for Baltimore last year, posting a 3.99 FIP over 62 Major League innings, while struggling mightily in Triple-A. Look for other owners to over pay and spend your resources elsewhere. The 25-year-old righty threw 206.1 innings, going 16-7 with a 3.71 ERA. There are some troubling signs, namely a favorable batting average on balls in play (.269) and strand rate (80.5%) that underlie a 4.42 FIP. On the other hand, his 14.2% home run per fly ball rate rate was unfriendly, though that’s an issue he’s had in prior major league stints as well. These negatives only serve to bury the positives, however – Tillman mostly maintained his 2012 velocity spike and improved both his strikeout rate and walk rate in the second half. Most importantly, he trimmed the fly ball rate, something he needs to do even further in 2014. One of the most profitable starters in 2013, don’t pay an expectant price this year but feel comfortable drafting him for an ERA around four and 7.5 strikeouts per nine. Tillman has now thumbed his nose at FIP and xFIP for three seasons straight, outperforming these predictors by a wide margin despite an underwhelming strikeout rate and a fly ball tendency. He has increased his ground ball rate for three seasons straight, and his home run per fly ball rate was almost halved in 2014 at 8.3% versus 14.2% in 2013. His 17.2% strikeout rate represents a pretty significant step back from his 21.2% rate in 2013, and ranks him up there among the no-bat-missing likes of Bartolo Colon and Jason Vargas. However -- his second half shouldn't be overlooked where his strikeout rate was 21.5%, his walk rate dropped from 9.3% to 5.1%, his ERA  dropped from 4.11 to 2.33 (FIP 4.49 to 3.38, respectively) and his WHIP went from 1.39 to 1.01 -- all while holding opposing batters to a .216/.258/.341 slash line. Concerns that his breakout was just an 86-inning flash in the pan dissipated in 2013 and 2014, as Tillman kept his ERA under four in both seasons while pitching over 200 innings in each. Tillman's peripherals were unimpressive, but given that he seemed to managing the feat for hundreds of innings over two seasons made Tillman appear to be an exception. Things came crashing down in 2015 for Tillman, though. His strikeout and walk rates, never impressive to begin with, were both worse than in his successful previous seasons. His home run per fly ball rate was worse than 2014, but still much better than in 2013, when he finished with a 3.71 ERA. In 2015, Tillman's ERA was a terrible 4.99. The main problem was his batting average on balls in play, which jumped to .293 after being below .270 during the previous two seasons. While Tillman may have some low BABIP skill, in 2015 he was right about average, which probably indicates some expected regression to the mean. It is not as if 2012-2014 do not matter, but though 2015 was probably below his true talent level, it is still probably a significant sign of what Tillman is as a pitcher. His repertoire is decent, but not standout. Tillman has some value in deeper leagues, where he might even be undervalued. Injuries might have played a role in his problems, but one should be careful when overcompensating for such things.
