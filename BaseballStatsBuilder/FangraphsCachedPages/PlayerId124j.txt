                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>How you feel about Eddie Butler may depend on your outlook in life. If you're an optimist, you will likely note that he has twice been a top-100 prospect. If you're a pessimist, you will likely note that through 95.1 innings of his major league career, he has walked more batters (49) than he has struck out (47). Butler throws his changeup fairly often (12.6% of the time last season), but unfortunately has been really bad at throwing it. Perhaps the fact that he only has a six mph difference between his fastball and his changeup has been the problem. Either way, last season, only three pitchers in the majors accumulated less value on their changeups than did Butler on his (-8.0). Does this mean that Butler is destined to be a flameout? No, not necessarily. But at this juncture, it's hard to point to the positive things on his statistical resume. Basically all of his statistics are horrifying, outside of his ground ball rate, and even that ground ball rate was lower than it was in his limited 2014 sample. Butler will likely have a shot at the fifth spot in the rotation, but there's certainly no spot waiting for him. He's going to have to earn it, and after his performance last season, he'll have to be extra convincing. (Paul Swydan)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Making a top prospect list is always a feather in the cap of a young player, but there isn't a top prospect list in the world that can save you once you make it to The Show.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
