        <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The Comeback Player of the Year may not be down with FIP -- yeah you know we -- but that doesn't mean that he's a lock for a near-five ERA next year. He sits right at the crux of That Which We Cannot Measure. You can measure his 85 mph fastball velocity, yes, but you can't (yet) measure exactly what that fastball looks like to opposing hitters. By being super tall and releasing the ball closer to home plate than anyone in baseball, and living up in the zone more than most, <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/blogs/chris-young-go-look-at-the-research-again/">Young is making the most of what he has</a>. It's hard to get your hands around a fastball high and tight, and most of today's hitters are low-ball hitters, so he's zigging while the league zags. Of course, he still goes against all that we "know" about statistics and pitching -- he has one of the worst ground-ball rates in baseball, and his strikeout minus walk rate is almost half league average -- so he represents substantial risk. He's likely to take a short deal to fill in for a starter some time this spring, so treat him the same way as the major league teams: cautiously put him on the back end of your deep league team if the price and fit is right. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Since the peripherals are so bad, you have to be cautious with the 35-year-old with an 85 mph fastball. But he still has his height, pitching philosophy, and seeming ability to beat those metrics so far in his career -- and all of these things speak well to his potential usefulness in deep fantasy leagues.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
        <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The 37-year-old Chris Young has found a way to make his 87 mph fastball work in the majors. The formula is simple: fly balls. Tons and tons of them.  Since 2002, 620 pitchers have thrown over 300 innings and Young has the lowest ground ball rate of any of them at 26%. Reliever Tyler Clippard is the next closest at 28%. All those fly balls usually only mean two results, an out or a home run. He has found a way to maintain a below league average home runs per fly ball and therefore has a career ERA about one run less than his ERA estimators. While the ERA has been lower, it's still not great. He is a tough player to roster because he just doesn’t strike many players out and may get a few starts skipped being the Royals fifth starter. I see him only being useful in two-start weeks in deeper leagues. Owners should have an idea what to expect from Young and if that works in their leagues, use him. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Chris Young is a fairly boring pitcher which has made a niche for himself by not giving up any ground balls. He is not exciting otherwise and has limited fantasy implications.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
