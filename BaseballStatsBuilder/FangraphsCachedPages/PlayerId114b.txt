                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The pieces that led to McHugh's breakout in 2014 were still around in 2015. He still abandoned the sinker and the change that got rocked so often in Colorado and New York. He still threw the high four-seamer in order to change eye levels and set up his big curveball, and he still went to the cutter more often than any other starting pitcher in baseball not named Dan Haren. So the road map was the same, and yet the results weren't quite as good. Some of it might have been luck, as his batting average on balls in play went from .257 to .310, but even the pitcher would say that he wasn't as effective in his sophomore season. It was the cutter whose whiff rate dropped the most, and when I asked him about it midseason, McHugh admitted he was having a hard time with it. Normally able to vary the depth on the pitch, he found he couldn't get the same depth to righties in 2015. Even in his better months, he was two inches short of the drop he saw in 2014. Should the 28-year-old get that version of his cutter back, he might be able to recover some of the lost strikeouts next year. As is, though, it's a remarkably good outcome for a guy with an 89 mph fastball that was once on waivers. Even 2015's version was useable in most leagues. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>He probably wasn't ever as good as his stellar re-debut with the Astros in 2014, but Collin McHugh has three good pitches and a great sense of how to use them together. Sure, below-average velocity and a finicky cutter limit his upside. But when they mesh, he can add a strikeout per inning to good command and be a cheap second-shelf pitcher.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
