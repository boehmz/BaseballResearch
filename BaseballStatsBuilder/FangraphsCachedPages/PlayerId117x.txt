                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Jarred Cosart threw over 1000 pitches last year, in four or five different flavors depending on your favorite classification system. Not one of those flavors got average whiff rates. Sure, the fact that he throws a 95 mph cut fastball is interesting. And yes, his curveball has ten inches of drop and six inches of cut. That curve is a behemoth that led to decent strikeout rates in the minor leagues -- but only decent. And we haven't yet started talking about his command, which is poor. Legendarily poor, even -- his 14.2% walk rate would have led baseball if he qualified, by three percent. Or maybe we have started talking about his command. Maybe it's just command separating Cosart from better numbers across the board. Because batters have to swing at a curve to really take advantage of that kind of movement, and the best way to make batters swing at a curve is to put them in counts where they have to swing. And Cosart does get good ground-ball rates on most of his pitches. If there was some way he could iron out his issues, get batters to swing and miss at the curve, and use the rest of his arsenal for grounders... That's a lot of ifs, which collectively make Cosart a deep dynasty league pickup at best, a guy to monitor, a name to remember. (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/enosarris">Eno Sarris</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Even in his better games during his debut, Jarred Cosart struggled to command his pitches. Until he starts to win that struggle, he'll forever stymie your eye test. The curveball! The velocity! All for naught.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>What do you get when a pitcher's flaws are equal to their strengths? You might get something like Jarred Cosart. He throws 90% cutters and curves, and by shape and velocity of those pitches, you'd think he was an ace. The 94 mph cutter is six-to-eight inches different from your average four-seamer, horizontally. The 79 mph curve had the <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/leaders.aspx?pos=all&stats=pit&lg=all&qual=y&type=12&season=2014&month=0&season1=2014&ind=0&team=0&rost=0&age=0&filter=&players=0&sort=11,a">second-most drop in baseball</a> in 2014. Considering the general movements and velocities of those two pitches, you might think that he has all he needs to get the through the lineup multiple times. The problem is, the two-seamer never took. The change has been okay in small doses, but he doesn't seem to trust it. And the flaw that is equal to the strengths: he cannot command the ball. Sure, his walk and zone rates approached average last year -- and that's probably why the Marlins traded for him -- but he doesn't hit the catchers' mitt even when he's not walking guys. That's how his two pitches with good velocity and movement are both sub-par by whiff rates. That's how a 94 mph cutter and a big old honking curve ball aren't enough yet for Cosart to break out. Whether the flaws triumph over the strengths or vice versa is probably up the coaching staff in Miami. But at 24 years old, there are only so many more caps that we can write recommending Cosart as a late-round deep-league sleeper and general pitcher to be aware of. At some point, we have to have faith in one side or the other. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>If Jarred Cosart can't break out in Miami, we can finally stop writing upbeat player caps about his velocity and movement. Sometimes lack of command is a big enough flaw to sink even the most major league of arsenals.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Cosart only threw 70 innings last year, but it was another 70 that point toward a future in the bullpen. He doesn't strike nearly enough guys and worse yet, he walks too many. Usually the super-low strikeout guys at least avoid the self-inflicted pain of free passes. Cosart has just 1.4 strikeouts per walk in 310 major league innings. So far, he has consistently outrun those skills en route to relatively solid 3.54 ERA. He does keep the ball down and while they were an issue last year (1.3 home runs per nine), he doesn't usually allow many homers (0.6 career). His changeup had been good until 2015, so he has a reverse platoon split, but his pluses just aren't enough. It's easy to see a really strong second act in the bullpen. Again, there were a lot of scouting reports that had him relieving from jump street and it's not hard to imagine mid-90s, a strong curve, and tons of groundballs playing well in the bullpen.Â (<a href="http://thespguide.com/">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Cosart is out here just looking to get his first 2.0 strikeout to walk ratio and until that point, it's hard to find a format where you'd want him. There just aren't many starters succeeding with a mid-teens strikeout rate, so he might soon find himself in the bullpen, which is where many scouts eventually saw him landing anyway.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Cosart, a former top-50 prospect, has been traded three times in his career, spending time in the majors with the Astros, Marlins, and Padres. Perhaps the trades are a product of that former-prospect shine (some team finally thinking they can unlock untapped potential). Despite a roughly league-average career ERA of 3.92, Cosart has severely overperformed on his 4.29/4.42 career FIP/xFIP. His career 5.71 strikeouts per nine and 4.41 walks per nine leave much to be desired, causing some have to feel that his future may rest in the bullpen, which looks like a better fit for a couple reasons. First, he has never been particularly durable, injuring his hamstring, groin, and elbow in 2016. Second, while he possesses unsatisfying strikeout totals, he has shown above-average velocity paired with plus movement on his cutter and curve. This two-pitch mix could give him a recipe for success despite poor command. However, to say that the Padres rotation looked unstable would be putting it lightly, so it appears that Cosart will once again be in the rotation. At the moment he is not worth rostering in anything but the very deepest of leagues. (Joe Douglas)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>While he appears destined for the bullpen, the Padres rotation is unstable enough that he will likely start again in 2017.
