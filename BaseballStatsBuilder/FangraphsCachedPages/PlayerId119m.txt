                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>So far, Chase Anderson has been a little bit less than the sum of his parts. His changeup -- which he can manipulate to be bigger or smaller depending on the situation -- has been always above-average and sometimes plus by whiff rates. His curve is solidly above-average in both whiff and ground-ball rates. His fastballs are almost exactly average, after some improvement in the two-seamer last year. But despite what should be an average (or better) arsenal, he hasn't managed to put up an average season yet. After nearly three hundred innings, we may have to consider that average represents his upside. Two things offer a bit of hope for the future. For one, he showed better velocity after coming back from injury in the second half, and he boosted his strikeout rate as a result. And for two, the added use of the sinker should help him better his ground-ball and home-run rates one more time. Anderson is already 28, and it's possible the sinker robbed his changeup of some effectiveness last year, but in deep leagues, he's still a safe arm with no hype surrounding him. Even in Milwaukee -- at least his role is safer on the rebuilding squad. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>This twenty-eight year old still hasn't put all the pieces together, and there's still some hope, even in his new home. A good changeup and curve combo, with some added refinement in his fastballs, could yet produce an average (or better) season. Deep leaguers should consider him as a cheap, somewhat stable innings-eater at worst.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>2016's home run surge was especially harsh on Anderson. After giving up homers at near league-average rates in 2015, he gave up over 1.6 per nine innings in 2016, nearly 42% higher than the rest of the league. Unfortunately, we can't chalk this up to plain old bad homer-to-fly ball luck. While a couple ticks above the league average, the real culprit is that Anderson suddenly started giving up way more fly balls. His 41% fly ball rate wasn't just a career high, it ranked 15th-worst in the league among pitchers throwing at least 150 innings. He gave up more fly balls on his curve, devastating change, and marginalized two-seamer. Compounding his homer issue was a stark increase in walk rate. Anderson fell behind more often, in part because his zone percentage decreased, but also because hitters chased less frequently. In particular, Anderson's curve and change, his two best pitches, induced fewer swings outside the zone by a significant margin in 2016. We've now seen Anderson toss over 400 innings but without the results that his relatively deep arsenal might deserve. Most alarmingly, his loss of control and rise in fly balls suggest he's trending in the wrong direction. He's firmly a deep-league play or streamer in standard leagues. (Rylan Edwards)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Anderson has given us glimpses of serious upside in the past. However, three years into his career, he's not only yet to put it all together but is starting to show signs of an eroding skill set.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
