The belief in Shields paid off in spades as he ran rough shod over the American League in 2011. He decreased the usage of his fastball by 10 percent and implemented his curveball eight percent more; helping him keep hitters off balance and going deeper into games, resulting in a league high 11 complete games. He’s not likely to duplicate that type of success but maintains a very good strikeout rate and is a workhorse, ranking sixth in total innings pitched over the past five years. That durability combined with his high number of whiffs places him just below the very top tier of fantasy pitchers. (Erik Hahmann)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>He’s one of the more reliable arms in the game, and the amount of strikeouts he racks up helps offset the home runs he allows. He enters his age-31 season with a streak of six straight seasons over 200 innings and is fresh off setting a new career high in strikeout rate (23.6%). But the appropriate adage here is perhaps: "Reliable pitchers are reliable until they aren't anymore." Or, in other words, James Shields' future is just as uncertain as any other pitcher on the Royals' staff. He will assume the ace role for them, and should easily be their top pitcher in 2013, but he could have a repeat of 2010 batting average on balls in play insanity that resulted in a 5.18 ERA, or the <a href="http://www.royalsreview.com/2012/12/10/3750080/james-shields-royals-ace">growing concerns</a> about his arm angle fluctuations and declining Zone% could portend a serious injury. He is also leaving a pitcher's park and a great defense for a relatively neutral park and a not-as-great defense, but some have suggested the impact on his numbers <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/fantasy/index.php/james-shields-in-a-new-ballpark-and-divisio/">could be minimal</a> (parks affect different players in different ways, after all). All said, James Shields, given all the hubbub surrounding him the last two seasons, will draw a lot of interest in most fantasy drafts, and his stat sheet shows why. He has the potential for another 200 IP, 3.50 ERA season or even better. But do not pay more than you would for any other 31-year-old pitcher with a history of success and a few legitimate concerns. (<a href="https://twitter.com/BradleyWoodrum">Bradley Woodrum</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>James Shields had a productive six seasons as the workhorse of the Rays rotation, and now he will serve as the Royals' ace. In a deep league, he can be many teams' ace, but keep in mind his age -- 31 years old -- and his unkind change in park and defense.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>James Shields showed up for another great season in 2013. He ended it with a 3.15 ERA which was lower than his all his ERA estimators (3.47 FIP, 3.72 xFIP and 3.85 SIERA). The difference doesn't really surprise me -- the Royals have the league's best defense and as a team they had a team ERA–FIP of -.38. If Shields' ERA regresses in 2014, it will probably be because the defense regresses. Both his strikeouts per nine (8.8 to 7.7) and walks per nine (2.3 to 2.7) got worse compared to his 2012 values. The strikeouts don't concern me too much, since 7.7 K/9 is his career average. Additionally, his swinging strike rate was only down from 10.2% to 9.8%. The value I didn't like was the walk rate jump. Shields gets most of his value from not walking batters. If he begins walking batters, his value will decline quickly. The only other issue I have with the 32-year-old is the mileage on the arm. He has thrown over 200 innings for seven straight seasons. Pitchers eventually break down and so will Shields at some point. He should produce double digit wins, 200 Ks and a 3.50 ERA... if he stays healthy again. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>James Shields has been a steady performer over the past few seasons. I don't see the performance changing unless father time finally finds him.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Shields's fantasy value will be determined by who he ends up signing with this offseason. But you can estimate his value independent of his home park, to an extent. The first area to look and the greatest of concern is his strikeout rate. It has been declining since 2012 going from 8.8 per nine to 7.7 K/9 to 7.1 K/9. With his ability to stay healthy and pitch a ton of innings, his strikeout total has still gone from 223 to 196 to 180. The reason for his decline is not a drop in fastball velocity (steady around 92 mph), but from using his change less (28% to 25% to 22%) and it being less effective (21% swinging strike rate to 19% SwStr% to 16% SwStr%). He has offset the decline in strikeouts by posting a low 1.7 walks per nine rate in 2014. Over the last couple of seasons, his ERA (~3.20) has been below his ERA estimators (~3.60) because of an above average Royals offense. Without knowing which team signs him, expect 14 wins, 170 K, 3.60 ERA, and a 1.25 WHIP. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>James Shields is a predictable pitcher with the only wart being a declining strikeout rate. His value could change some depending on which team he finally signs with.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Big Game James. What a wonky year, man. You know that, in the 2015 incarnation of Steamer's projections, James Shields's projection had the highest reliability score of any starting pitcher? Understandably, too, as the trend seemed pretty clear: several years of remarkable consistency, but heading into his mid-30s, with strikeouts starting to taper off and minor fluctuations elsewhere. Then BLAM! He pulls a swift one on ya and posts arguably his worst season. A few things stick out like a sore thumb: his home run per fly ball rate spiked despite trivial changes to his batted ball breakdown. It explains a lot of everything that went wrong, because if you assume HR/FB is more luck than skill -- that is, if you subscribe to xFIP rather than FIP -- then 2015 wasn't so different. Except it still was, what with all those extra strikeouts and walks. Shields avoided the zone like the plague and his contact rate soared. That doesn't mean hitters offered at pitches outside the zone more frequently, however. It's this conundrum -- the fewest-swings, least-contact, fewest-pitches-in-the-zone conundrum -- that helps explain this Shieldsian oddity. Your guess is as good as ours as to which Shields you'll see in 2016. Perhaps the Padres catalyzed a paradigm shift when Shields joined the team. Perhaps it was a personal choice. Or, perhaps, it was just good ol' statistical variance that occurs every now and then. Whatever happens in 2016, it probably won't get much worse than it did last year. Still, he's a risky top-40 starter. (Alex Chamberlain)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>If you're scared of James Shields this year, we understand. There's room for him to rebound, but his newfound volatility makes him merely a top-40 consideration among starting pitchers.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
