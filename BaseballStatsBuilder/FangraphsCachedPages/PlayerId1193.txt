                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Kennedy was never going to be as good as he was in 2011, but in the past two seasons, his value took a brisk turn for the worse. His home run rate rose to a dangerous level, and it showed in his ERA and FIP. With Arizona's surplus of pitching, there was no reason for them to be loyal to the USC product, and to their credit they weren't -- they shipped him off to San Diego towards the end of the season. His home run rate didn't recover in the 57.1 innings he tossed for the Fathers, but over the course of a full season, it should. That could be all Kennedy needs to recover his value, as his strikeout rate remains healthy. For the past few years, the Padres' biggest problem has been consistency in its starting rotation in the early parts of each season. With Kennedy in the fold that will be less of a problem in 2014, and if the team is able to better settle in thanks to the continuity, that could be a boon for Kennedy as well. Help me, help you, etc. As such, Kennedy is a fantastic sleeper pick this season.<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Thanks to his tendency to give up a lot of fly balls, Chase Field wasn't exactly the best fit for Kennedy. But now he's ticketed for Petco Park, there's a pretty decent chance that Kennedy recovers his fantasy value.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The move to from Arizona to San Diego helped Kennedy become a valuable fantasy asset. The biggest change was the decline in home runs. In 2012, he gave up 1.2 homers per nine innings, and in 2013 he gave up 1.3 HR/9. In 2014, that rate dropped to a nice 0.7. The home change was expected, but the unexpected change was a 1.5 mph increase in fastball velocity (90.3 mph to 91.8 mph). With the increase, his strikeout rate jumped up to 9.3 per nine. A key for his value going into 2015 will be if he can keep the strikeouts up or have them drop to the near 8 K/9 level he showed from 2010 to 2013. I wouldn't bet on it staying up. He will be 30 years old and will likely see his velocity decline. Value him for his pre-2014 production and just consider it gravy if he over produces. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Ian Kennedy saw an improvement in his value by pitching half his games in San Diego and putting on velocity. Even though his home run and strikeout numbers improved, be leery of the strikeout values staying up.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Ian Kennedy cost the Royals all that money as well as a draft pick, which seems folly after last year's work, but he is likely poised for a bounce back of some order. Kennedy got killed by the long ball last year with a 17.2% home run per fly ball rate, which was well more than double what it was the year prior and about 60% higher than his career average. That will almost assuredly improve. Perhaps the bigger problem is that he allows a healthy number of fly balls, which can exacerbate a little bad luck with the rate at which your fly balls leave the yard. He also allows a healthy number of line drives, and that has been the case for three years running. That limits the extent of his potential bounce back. But he does well in the areas in which he has the most control, his strikeout and walk rates. He had a healthy strikeout minus walk rate of 17.1% last year, and his swinging strike rate has basically been in double digits for four years running. Those skills and the absence of horrible luck make Kennedy a good bet to be a fair bit better than he was last year. But his issues limiting hard contact, and the tougher league he faces now, may mitigate his regression to the mean.  (Brett Talley)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Kennedy had a wildly unsustainable home run per fly ball rate of 17.2% last year, and the inevitable regression in that department along with good strikeout and walk skills make him a good candidate to bounce back on some level. But issues limiting hard contact may mitigate his improvement.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Kennedy has elite swing and miss stuff, which has led to double digit swinging strike rates and the 11th-most strike outs over the past six seasons. Unfortunately, he has given up far too many home runs over the course of his career, which leaves him in a precarious position in terms of value. This is made all the more extreme when you look at his second half numbers from 2016, in which he dropped his home run rate significantly while maintaining his strikeout numbers. As a result, he had a 2.66 ERA over his final thirteen starts, during which he allowed seven home runs in 81.1 innings. Granted, this success came on the heels of a June/July period in which he gave up 18 home runs and 36 earned runs in nine starts. Towards the end of 2016, Kennedy jumped up towards a 68% fastball usage rate, at the expense of his changeup. This is particularly interesting due to his changeup’s ability to induce ground balls, which helped offset his fastball’s well above average fly ball rate. Instead, it appears he is doubling down on fly balls, which could make him more susceptible to home runs in the future. (Andrew Perpetua)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Kennedy is tantalizingly close to becoming an ace, but his propensity for home runs appears to be insurmountable. At this point, he is more aptly described as a 5th or 6th starter in most league formats.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
