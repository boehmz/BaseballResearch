                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Nelson made his (brief) MLB debut in 2013 but spent much of the year in the minors and split that time between Double-A and Triple-A. The right-hander isn't flashy but he has a strong frame and should be a durable mid-rotation starter for the Brewers. He flashed good velocity with his fastball and his slider also showed promise. On the other hand, he used his change up about 2% of the time in those limited innings, and that was a trend that also followed him through the minors. Nelson may have to open the 2014 season back in Triple-A if he's unable to wrestle a rotation spot away from the Brewers' pair of sophomore hurlers: Tyler Thornburg and Wily Peralta. If the changeup doesn't develop, the bullpen is a possibility, too. (Marc Hulet)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Nelson should eventually be talented enough to provide 200-220 innings a year for the Brewers but there's enough depth in the upper minors and the majors to keep Nelson in Triple-A for at least part of the season.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Jimmy Nelson enters 2015 as the sixth man on the starting pitcher totem pole in Milwaukee -- barring an injury, he'll likely open the year in the minors. FIP suggests the right-hander's 4.93 ERA was about a run too high so there is reason for optimism with the young pitcher. He has a solid repertoire, sturdy build and limited the walks during his rookie season. He needs to improve the command of his fastball, especially considering he's more or less a two-pitch starter with the use of his changeup coming in around 2-3%. Nelson is a great player to target in keeper leagues but, given an opportunity, could be a solid performer in NL-only leagues with an eye towards becoming mixed-league relevant in 2016. (Marc Hulet)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Nelson doesn't have a clear path to an opening day roster spot, which means he's more likely to be relevant in the second half of the season. His success in 2015 will rely on improved command of his fastball, and perhaps a more reliable third pitch. He may not be mixed league relevant until 2016.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Nelson continued his growth toward a mid-rotation arm, but did so by further smothering righties to cover his persistent trouble with lefties. Nelson posted the sixth-worst OPS against lefties (.876) among the 68 righties who faced at least 300 lefties. However, there was still a big improvement that bodes well for this primary problem: he developed a third pitch that might help him succeed against southpaws. Nelson's curveball replaced the changeup and did so very well with a .533 OPS in 53 plate appearances. The fastball just keeps getting hammered. The only area of the zone where it found some success against lefties was the upper third. He did throw more up there in 2015 -- going from 41% to 45% which was 12th-most among right-handers (minimum 90 PA) -- but maybe he needs to be up there at least half the time. Lefties are really the only glaring issue for Nelson. Of course, just because he only has one issue instead of a laundry list of 'em doesn't mean it will be an easy fix. Take a shot late if you do. (Paul Sporer)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Nelson had a consolidation season as he fine-tuned his strengths and rode them to a league average season. It was his first full season so it's definitely something to build on; he's a reasonable late-round flier with a tinge of upside.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Armed with four pitches,  Nelson relies heavily on a sinker to generate excellent ground ball rates that have hovered throughout his career on either side of 50%. But the tall righty struggled in 2016 due in large part to a loss of command, particularly against lefties. His 13% walk rate against southpaws was fourth worst in the league. And because he doesn't strike many out either, his K%-BB% was the third lowest out of 101 qualified pitchers. While he fared better against righties, his performance was nothing to write home about, and unfortunately, that kind of production suggests whatever upside he may have possessed entering 2016 has likely sailed. Now to say his problems are all walk-related doesn't quite tell the whole story. Nelson's 2016 zone percentage was actually the highest of his career and well above league average. It was actually that hitters chased pitches out of the zone at career-low rates, forcing him into hitter's counts. And in those counts, Nelson served up meatballs, evidenced by the 23rd-highest hard-hit rate in the league. Ground balls alone aren't enough to make this lanky worm-burner mixed-league relevant. (Rylan Edwards)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Nelson's struggles with command and contact management only exacerbate troubles caused by his inability to strike batters out. Barring the development of some swing-and-miss stuff, he has no value in standard leagues.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
