                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>Petit has looked brilliant in the minor leagues at times, specifically in 2008 when he posted a fantastic 2.88 FIP in Triple-A, thanks to a 10.05 K/9 and 1.20 BB/9 – good for a crazy 8.38 K/BB rate. Those gaudy numbers earned Petit a shot in the Majors with Arizona in 2009. He still posted decent K and BB rates – his K/BB rate of 2.18 ranks in the top half of all starters with at least 80 innings pitched. Petit's problem in the Major Leagues has been the home run: in 17 starts and six relief appearances in 2009, he allowed 19 homers, resulting in a 1.91 HR/9. Petit's fly-ball percentage approaches 50%, and Major League hitters will make pitchers pay for fly balls with home runs. <br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>Petit was claimed by the Seattle Mariners in the offseason. If an extreme fly-baller can survive anywhere, it would be in spacious Safeco Field. Also, with Franklin Gutierrez and Ichiro Suzuki roaming the outfield, the Mariners are the perfect fit for Petit's unique talents. Still, the right-hander is probably not going to make the Mariners rotation out of camp, but he will definitely be a player to watch if he does crack the rotation in the event of an injury.  In Seattle, Petit could put up an ERA closer to 4.50, certainly better than his career mark of 5.50, and his strikeout rate should be good for a starting pitcher. Don't let Petit slip under the radar, especially if he lucks into a starting rotation spot. (Jack Moore) </td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>While many will point to Petit's strong 2013 campaign and talk about how pitching in AT&T Park and in the pitcher-friendly National League West can make even an average pitcher a worthwhile commodity, he seems more likely to be the real-life example of how the sun shines on dog's posterior at least once. He finished with a 3.56 ERA and while he had an impressive 2.84 FIP, his 3.48 xFIP seemed more indicative of how he was really pitching. He only made eight starts for the Giants last year and while his first four looked impressive, three of his last four were downright atrocious. He walks a tightrope with an 87 mph fastball, even if his offspeed stuff is good. Still, he'll get a chance to compete for a rotation spot in 2014 and should he earn one, fantasy owners who aren't careful are liable to walk away with the pitcher who is more closely represented by the career 5.20 ERA which he possesses. (Howard Bender)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Petit threw a complete-game shutout in which he allowed just one hit last year! However, his true talent reared its ugly head shortly thereafter. He'll get the chance to compete for a rotation spot next season, but fantasy owners should take more notice of his career 5.20 ERA than his numbers for 2013.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After years of having produced above-average -- and, in some cases, unconscionably good -- strikeout and walk rates at the minor league level, Petit has finally approximated those numbers in the majors. Between 2013 and 2014 with the Giants, he's recorded strikeout and walk rates of 27.4% and 5.0%, respectively, over 165 innings (roughly two-thirds of those in a starting capacity). During that same interval, only four qualified starters -- Yu Darvish, Matt Harvey, Clayton Kershaw, and Chris Sale -- have posted a strikeout- and walk-rate differential better than Petit's mark of 22.4%. Generally, K-BB% correlates strongly with run prevention. Indeed, the four aforementioned starters have produced a collective ERA about 40% better than league average. Petit, meanwhile, has recorded an ERA 7% worse than average. One explanation is that he's been a victim of batted-ball variance. A more likely alternative, however, is that his lack of arm speed -- he's produced an average fastball velocity of just 88.6 mph over the last two years -- is the culprit. There remains a possibility that Petit will become a competent starter in the major leagues. That contingency will wait till some point after opening day, however, as Giants manager Bruce Bochy has designed Petit as a "super reliever" for the club once again. (Carson Cistulli)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Owing to a lack of velocity, probably, Petit's fielding-independent numbers seem likely to outpace his actual run-prevention numbers. Probably isn't a candidate to provide even league-average run prevention as a starter, but may be useful in stretches at home.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Though his strikeout rate surge of 2013 and 2014 failed to stick, Petit's career revival is still quite remarkable. From a failed prospect, to not pitching in the majors in 2010 or 2011, to transforming into a very solid swingman, Petit has surprised us all. His curve ball has remained absolutely elite, generating a swinging strike rate over 25% now for three consecutive seasons. Unfortunately, his changeup has lost its whiff abilities and his high-80s fastball is no good. But he possesses good control and all those fly balls and pop-ups should suppress his batting average on balls in play. Petit now takes his soft-tossing ways to Washington where he will provide insurance for the youngsters at the back of their rotation and shore up their middle relief. (<a href="http://www.projectingx.com/" target="_blank">Mike Podhorzer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>With a ridiculous curve ball, Petit should continue to find success, whether as a starter or reliever. He's a solid addition to NL-Only rosters and could become a streaming option in mixed leagues if he finds himself making starts for the Nationals.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Petit, the purveyor of the Eno Sarris-trademarked "invisiball," wasn't at his best in 2016. Hitters murdered his fastball (.519 slugging percentage against) and cutter (.550 SLG). He possessed interesting splits. Righties had trouble with his main fastball - the so-called invisiball - and his curve. They feasted on his frequently-thrown cutter. Lefties clearly saw the invisiball just fine (.711 SLG), but they had some difficulty against the change. He rarely threw his cutter or curve to southpaws. Going forward, Petit needs to rediscover a means to limit left-handed hitters. He's used mainly as a long reliever so it's impractical for him to take a ROOGY approach. The cutter seems to be useless as anything more than a way to keep hitters off the one fastball. Throwing it less often could help. (Brad Johnson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Petit was pounded by left-handed hitters in 2016. While we're talking about a small sample, it seems like southpaws were seeing his fastball very well. He needs to work harder on being unpredictable.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
