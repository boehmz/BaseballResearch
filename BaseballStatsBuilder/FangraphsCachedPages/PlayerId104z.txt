If he did not have control problems, he would have likely cracked the Rays rotation a season or two sooner on the merit of his raw stuff. Archer has an easy mid-90s fastball, as well as a two-seam/sinker and slider combo that generate buckets of weak contact. But his 3.22 ERA in 2013 looked dim under the shadow of his pedestrian 4.07 FIP. If he can continue to keep the walk rate low, do not be surprised if he can also repeat or improve upon his success in the 2014 season. He might always have trouble with lefties if he doesn't improve his change up, though.                 <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Chris Archer jumped a major hurdle in 2014, finally demonstrating an ability to get left-handed batters out regularly. He added a new pitch, a sinker, that kept the bat off the barrel of lefty bats, and the results showed up in his overall line. His 3.33 ERA over 194 innings is the stuff of an above-average starter. His numbers against lefties improved enough to mask an uptick in walks and in-play average. Simply put, he was a better pitcher in 2014. He cut his home run rate in half, allowing just 12 homers for the year. Even if regression drives that number up, he still represents a strong starter option, though the rebuilding Rays don't make for the most attractive partner for those seeking wins. As he matures, Archer can better avoid the huge walk games that plagued him in 2014, when he issued four or more free passes seven times. The erudite hurler makes great use of the information available to him and is still young enough at 26 that an improvement in his command could be in the cards. Archer has the tools and mindset to take another step forward after his promising 2014 showed just what he can do. (Drew Fairservice)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>After making strides against left-handed batters in 2014, Chris Archer sets his sights on improving his walk rate to make 2015 the year he puts it all together to become a complete pitcher.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Entering the 2015 season, the Rays expected to count on Archer as their number three pitcher behind Alex Cobb and Drew Smyly. Instead, injuries vaulted Archer into the opening day spot, and he reciprocated by posting a Cy Young-contending 2015 season. Just 27 years old entering his 2016 campaign, Archer looks like nothing else if not an early candidate for Cy Young contention again. Despite showing career-best velocity, it was his slider and changeup that carried the weight of his huge season and caused a three point increase in his swinging strike rate. He can and does pump serious gas into the strikeout, but as long as his offspeed pitches perform on such a high level, it is hard to imagine Archer showing any signs of aging even if his velocity has already peaked. What's even better for Rays fans: He's signed to a team-friendly extension with team options through the 2021 season. (<a href="https://twitter.com/BradleyWoodrum">@BradleyWoodrum</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Archer is a newly-minted ace coming off his best season. He's got all the tools and time to put together another great season, or possibly even improve. If he's available in a fantasy league, he is worth early-draft consideration.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Yet Archer put on a clinic in the summer, cutting free passes nearly in half while maintaining his elite strikeout rate. Ultimately, he finished the 2016 season almost identically to his 5.2-WAR 2015 season... as long as you ignore the home run issues. The baseball gods chose a select few, both deserving and undeserving, to bear the brunt of the league-wide power surge; alas, he allowed home runs on 16% of fly balls after keeping that rate below league-average since his debut. Anything can happen — last year proved that in spades — but it's hard to believe Archer will suffer the same ill fate in 2017. Recall that the blossoming ace owned a reeeeal pretty 3.33 ERA validated by a 3.36 FIP and 3.47 xFIP prior to last year's Jekyll-and-Hyde results. All that said, his incredible second half didn't go unnoticed, and a sabermetric community that is savvier than ever recognizes his peripherals are legit. He's a virtual lock for excellence again — he may even take another step forward, should he sustain his sparkling second-half walk rate — and could sniff top-15 ADP among starting pitchers.
