                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Gray’s velocity was the reason he jumped up to the third-overall pick in 2013, and it propelled him quickly through the minor leagues to make his major league debut last season. He made nine starts in August and September before being shut down upon reaching an innings limit, and the results were mostly favorable, particularly by fielding independent metrics (5.53 ERA, 3.63 FIP, 3.84 xFIP). While his fastball has opened eyes, it’s his slider that will keep him viable as his command gets ironed out. Averaging 94.4 mph on his fastball would have put him 13th among big league starters had he accumulated enough innings to qualify, though overall his heater gave up the most damage of his three offerings. Though he limited walks respectably, hitters had little problem jumping on his fastball, which he has had trouble commanding to the corners of the zone. His slider was elite level, both in terms of whiffs and command, a pitch that can reach the upper-80s with tight downward and glove-side bite. His changeup lags behind the other two offerings in present and potential quality. Even without an above average third pitch, his big fastball and hard slider give him a starter’s repertoire. The command of his fastball will be the determining factor in how well he can do in the rotation. At present his inconsistent arm slot and delivery efficiency clouds the ceiling on his command, but I’m optimistic he will continue improving as he has over the last two years since being drafted. (Dan Farnsworth)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>If Gray can settle in with slightly below-average command, he’s a number three starter with the stuff he possesses. While he may lack raw athleticism, he does show potential growth in harnessing his fastball enough to flirt with a number two starter role, but it’s still a work in progress.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>When Gray went third overall to the Rockies back in 2013, it was a bit of a disappointment because seeing such a high-upside arm have to deal with the volatility of Coors Field felt like a bit of a waste. Flash forward three-plus seasons and those concerns still linger, but Gray looks to have an arsenal capable of thwarting the hitter's haven, or at least taming it enough to be a mixed-league arm in all formats. A 4.61 ERA doesn't necessarily inspire confidence, but it was more his road work that inflated the ERA. He had a 4.91 mark away from Coors, thanks to a nine earned run shellacking at St. Louis in just 3.3 innings. His 4.1 IP/6 ER dud at Philly didn't help, either. He still had a 4.30 ERA in Coors, but the 1.12 WHIP, 27% strikeout rate, and 5.2 K:BB ratio show just how well he pitched in the difficult environment. Because you can pitch that well and still wind up with a 4.30 ERA in Coors, Gray can't falter on the road like he did. In short, his off-days need to be better. They happen to every pitcher, but seven outings of 5+ ER won't work if he's going to break out. Let's not be too harsh on a rookie who showed flashes of greatness with an impressive 2016 that has us thirsty for more.
