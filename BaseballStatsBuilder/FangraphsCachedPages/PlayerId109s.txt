                <tr id="so5" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Fiers blasted through the Brewers minor league system in 2011 and was rewarded with 22 major league starts in 2012. The 27-year-old did as well as anybody could have expected, finishing the season with a 3.74 ERA and 3.09 FIP. Although Fiers is a soft-tosser (88.5 mph average), his secondary stuff has sharp and pronounced movement. Fiers is able to get to pitchers' counts and miss bats -- he recorded 9.5 strikeouts per nine behind an 8.3% swinging strike rate. However, much of Fiers's success has been chalked up to deception and there are concerns the home runs could start flying thanks to his soft stuff and inability to get ground balls (32.7% in 2012). Still, he's an intriguing option if he wins a start in the Brewers' bullpen thanks to his control (2.5 walk rate) and strikeout stuff. (Jack Moore)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Was Fiers's success in 2012 due to deception? This is the question many have after Fiers posted a 94 ERA- and 79 FIP- in his rookie season despite a mediocre fastball.                 <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Mike Fiers was here again, gone again, and back again over the last three years. And yet, if you look at his pitches by movement, velocity, and average location, there's not a lot of change. Well, okay, maybe there was an ill-advised move on the rubber early in 2013, and a few too many low fastballs late in 2013. Because when Fiers is going right, he's using his rising fastball (his fastball has two inches more ride to it than average) up in the zone to set up his excellent combo of changeup (16% whiffs) and curve (10% whiffs) with a decent cutter mixed in (8% whiffs). For his career, Fiers gets more whiffs than average on his fastball, too (8%). This, despite averaging around 89 mph on the pitch. It's cool, he's got command and that arsenal. The risk still comes from that fastball, though -- when he was bad, it was the pitch that went for home runs more than twice as often as his other pitches. And he works in Milwaukee. If the price is right (and since he's burned his owners before, it might be), take a chance that his strikeouts and walks can overwhelm the home runs. Remember the good times, but try not to pay for them. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Because his arsenal depends on throwing 88 mph fastballs up in the zone while playing in Milwaukee, there's considerable risk surrounding the smoke-less Fiers. The rest of his arsenal, and his command, says he's worth taking a shot on in any league, though.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Prior to 2012, Fiers looked like the definition of a Quad-A starter, but in his age-27 season, he proved he could handle major league hitting, at least until the league figured him out. The thing is, the league never really has. Outside of an injury-plagued 2013, Fiers has been a quality major league starter, and he shows no signs of slowing heading into his early 30s. Fiers was dealt to the Houston Astros in the Carlos Gomez trade at last year’s deadline, and he impressed his new fan base with a no-hitter in his fourth start with the club. A fly ball specialist with a sub-90 mph fastball, you wouldn’t think Fiers’ best fantasy tool is the strikeout, but indeed it is, as he’s struck out nearly a quarter of the batters he’s faced over his career. Houston is certainly not a good ballpark for a pitcher with the right-hander’s skillset, but neither was Milwaukee. Fiers’ biggest concern should be facing a designated hitter every start, which will result in fewer strikeouts, more walks, and more homers compared to a pitcher and pinch-hitters in the National League. The Astros are relying on Fiers this year, but you shouldn’t be. Take a flier on Fiers in the last quarter of your drafts and see if he can keep his rate stats in check and deliver you some cheap strikeouts. (Zach Sanders)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Mike Fiers does two things well: he strikes batters out, and he generates pop-ups, which can lead to a lower batting average on balls in play. Fiers also gives up a few too many homers, but if he can keep his ERA, and more importantly his WHIP, in an acceptable range -- say around 3.75 and 1.25, respectfully -- he’s a valuable back-third fantasy starter who can deliver some gems.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
