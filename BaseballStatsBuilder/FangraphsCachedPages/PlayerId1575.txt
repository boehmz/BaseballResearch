                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>A chronic underachiever in the Braves' farm system prior to reaching Triple-A, Morton went from the periphery of Atlanta's pitching plans to the heart of Pittsburgh's latest attempt to revamp its rotation. The 6’4’’ right-hander confounded scouts for years with impressive stuff but an aggravating lack of control. Something seemed to click for Morton in Triple-A, however, as he punched out three batters for every walk issued between 2008 and 2009. He packed his bags for the 'Burgh in June, as one of three prospects the Bucs acquired for Nate McLouth. In 18 starts with the Pirates, Morton posted a 1.55 K/BB. His sinking 91 mph fastball helped him post an above-average ground-ball rate, and his 79 mph curveball rated as 1.6 runs above average per 100 pitches. Morton's low-80s change-up was lackluster, though, and he often fell behind batters with a 52% first-pitch-strike rate.<br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>Morton has the ingredients to become a quality Major League starter, possessing a heater that runs in on the hands of righties and a sweeping breaking ball that baits batters into taking weak hacks. However, the 26-year-old needs to find a way to combat left-handed batters. In his Major League career, Morton has limited right-handers to a .594 OPS, but southpaws have scorched him for a .923 mark. He will also have to continue sharpening his control. Happily, Morton did show progress as he climbed the ladder to the big leagues. He allowed 4.8 walks per nine innings prior to AAA ball, but just 2.6 per nine frames at the highest level of the minors. With the Pirates in full-fledged rebuilding mode, Morton is almost assured a rotation spot in 2010. If he can pound the zone more often and hone that change-up, Morton could be an asset. Unfortunately, Morton has yet to strand baserunners at a better-than-average rate, contributing to ERAs that perennially betray his underlying metrics. He tends to pitch more to contact in higher-leverage situations, and the batted ball gods have not been kind to him in such contexts throughout his career. That makes Morton a wholly average starter who doesn't cut it in shallow leagues but makes for a decent late-round investment on draft day in NL-only leagues -- with upside, if he can stay healthy for once.
