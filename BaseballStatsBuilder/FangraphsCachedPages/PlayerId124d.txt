                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Traded to the A’s in the deal that sent Ben Zobrist to Kansas City at the deadline in 2015, Manaea debuted early last season and reeled off one of the better rookie pitcher performances of the year. The big lefty has a three pitch mix, relying heavily on a fourseam fastball that averaged 93 mph and a slider that managed a 20% swinging strike rate on it’s own. Manaea completed his rookie season throwing just under 150 innings (144 ?) with a 3.86 earned run average, seven victories, and a healthy amount of strikeouts (7.71 per nine innings), numbers all the more impressive when you realize he gave up 21 earned runs in his first 27 innings of the season. Like most young pitchers though, Manaea still has some hurdles to overcome. For one, he’ll need to solve right handed hitters, as 17 of his 20 home runs allowed came vs. righties. He’ll also need to find a way to pitch more effectively away from the large ballpark in Oakland, as his ERA suffered (4.99 away vs. 3.09 home) outside of the Coliseum thanks to a .457 slugging percentage allowed. Learning curves notwithstanding, Manaea has all the makings of a very solid pitcher starting as early as 2017. His clear improvements in the 2nd half (increased strikeout to walk ratio from 2.75 to 4.06; reduced HR/9 rate and WHIP) are positive signs that he adjusted well as the season wore on, and the fact that he lead all starting pitchers (min. 100 innings) last season in inducing swings (52%) indicates there may be quite a bit of developmental upside on the way (his 12% swinging strike rate ranked above notable strikeout pitchers Madison Bumgarner, Chris Sale, and Stephen Strasburg). Young pitchers are inherently risky as they navigate the inevitable adjustments that occur at the big league level, but Manaea is as good as any young pitcher to move towards the top of your 2017 sleeper list. (Trey Baughn)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Manaea has the pedigree and peripherals to take a step forward in 2017, so he may be a young pitcher you should target a round or two earlier than the masses. His underlying skills suggest an uptick in strikeouts is coming, and with added command and slight improvements versus right handed hitters, he’s in a good position to be a breakout pitcher this season.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
