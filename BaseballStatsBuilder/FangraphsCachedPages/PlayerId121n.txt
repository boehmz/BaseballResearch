                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>There are lots of reasons to like Jacob deGrom better than his projections, which are (in part, at least) based on some mediocre minor league strikeout rates. You see, deG was a different pitcher in 2014 than he'd ever been before. As a college shortstop that sometimes closed and then had Tommy John surgery early in his pro career, he entered 2012 as an older prospect with one pitch. Since then, he's added a changeup with <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/blogs/why-jacob-degrom-is-better-than-we-thought-hed-be/">Johan Santana's grip</a>, a curveball with Frank Viola's grip, and a faster slider under pitching coach Dan Warthen's tutelage. Now the man with the hair has four pitches that rate as average or above by whiffs. Considering his four-seamer got almost double the average four-seamer whiff rate, it's an impressive collection. And it makes his 11.7% swinging strike rate -- eighth best in the league among pitchers that threw at least 140 innings last year -- completely believable. Will his strand rate and homer rate regress? Probably. But there is evidence that pitchers have more control over these rates than previously thought, as SIERA research has shown a link between strikeout rate and strand rate. Tick his projected strikeout and strand rates up a bit, and his homer rate down, and you've got a low-threes ERA with a strikeout per inning. That described 20 pitchers last year. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Any analysis that ends up with Jacob deGrom outside the top 25 is ignoring huge strides the pitcher has made in the last two years. Pay more than projections-based auction value and rankings would have you pay, but keep your hat on. He can't just replicate last season's work.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>DeGrom took a big step forward in 2015 adding more whiffs and more first pitch strikes, which led to even better strikeout and walk rates. That along with a full season's worth of work pushed deGrom into the top 10 in RotoGraphs' end of season starter values. At this point, we can safely ignore his lackluster strikeout rates in the minors. As for how he potentially gets better in 2016, the easiest way would probably be two or three more starts. It's hard to project a guy to further improve on a 22.2% strikeout minus walk rate that ranked seventh among qualified starters. So to go from top 10 to top five, he'll need to repeat what he did last year over a few extra starts. As for his potential downside, his batting average on balls in play is the easiest thing to point out. Mike Podhorzer has noted that his batted ball profile doesn't exactly lend itself to a lower-than-average BABIP. But a lot of it will depend on whether the Mets defense manages to be average again. If it is, deGrom is less susceptible to BABIP regression. We're really splitting hairs here when it comes to whether deGrom gets better or worse. Him finishing top five among starters is possible but not necessarily likely, and him finishing outside the top 20 starters is possible but not likely.  (Brett Talley)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Any concerns about deGrom's lack of strikeouts in the minors were officially put to rest last year as deGrom added velocity and whiffs and ended up with the seventh best strikeout rate among qualified starters. He finished top 10 in fantasy value among starters, and a similar finish in 2016 is reasonable to expect. A repeat in rate stats plus two or three extra starts could push him towards the top five, and a little batted ball regression could push him towards the back end of the top 20.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
