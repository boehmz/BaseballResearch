                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The pieces are there for greatness. A 95 mph fastball. A history of good command. A diving, fading splitter of death. Kevin Gausman has not yet, however, been great. The first problem is that he hasn't been able to show a good breaking pitch. An 8% whiff rate on his slider is well below the 13% average, and he gives up more homers per pitch on the slider than any other pitch he throws. A .225 isolated slugging percentage allowed... very little horizontal movement... very little velocity (82 mph)... the slider's probably not going to be any good as it is. Another problem is that his team won't let him learn a cutter, which might his best option going forward. There's a chance that he could just ditch the slider altogether and be the rare fastball/change/splitter pitcher, though. His regular change is flatter and harder, and if he figured out how to use two fastballs and two changeups correctly, he could still be great. Perhaps more high fastballs could do the trick. The problem is that his overall swinging strike rate and standard peripherals were underwhelming last year, and you may end up having to bid against others that fell in love with his velocity. If you can pay sleeper prices, go ahead. The pieces are there. But if you have to pay as if this puzzle has been put together, maybe it's best to pass.                 <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Gausman finished the 2016 season with a career high in innings (179.2), nearly one strikeout per nine innings pitched (8.7 K/9), and his highest percentage of swings induced outside the strike zone (35%) since his 2013 debut, but most fantasy owners will base any 2017 optimism upon 76 of his innings between August and October that helped propel him to a 3.10 second half earned run average. What fueled the second half improvement? More than anything else, it looks like a higher usage of his split changeup, which was always hailed as Gausman’s bread and butter pitch when coming up through the minor leagues. Gausman not only increased his split usage year over year, but his reliance upon the pitch increased as the season went on, spiking in September. Throwing a great pitch more often seems like an obvious step in the right direction for Gausman heading into 2017, but even with a development jump it appears his potential for true breakout will still be tied to his ability to suppress the home run (at least 1.36 home runs per nine innings in each of the last two seasons). Strangely, his home run issue has been much more of a problem away from Camden Yards, so keep an eye on his home/road splits as he takes the mound in 2017. Gausman will pitch all of 2017 at the age of 26 which, combined with small but tangible improvements in pitch usage should imply at least the possibility of a larger step forward coming soon.
