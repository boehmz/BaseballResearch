                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>Any way you want to cut it, Greinke was the most valuable commodity in baseball in 2009. The AL Cy Young award winner led all starters in FIP and WAR, while ranking fourth in the Majors with a 4.75 K/BB ratio. Greinke's K rate and fastball velocity have skyrocketed since his return to the mound in 2007. Once sitting at 90 mph while whiffing about six batters per nine frames, Greinke now throws 94 mph gas and posted a 9.5 K/9 in 2009. That fastball was plenty productive, ranking in the top 10 in value per 100 pitches thrown. But that's just the tip of the iceberg for the Royals righty. His mid-80s slider ranked in the top five among starters, and his low-70s curveball was an above-average offering, as well. Greinke got ahead of hitters often, with a 62% first-pitch strike rate.<br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>After hiding in plain sight for a few seasons as an underappreciated ace, Greinke's name is now firmly in the mind of every baseball fan on the planet. The 26-year-old has all the skills to terrorize the AL for years to come. However, owners shouldn't expect him to be Pedro Martinez circa 2000 year in and year out. That's just not feasible for any starter. There's nothing fluky in the least bit about Greinke and his sinister stuff, but expect some regression to the mean in 2010. He surrendered a home run on just 4.5% of the fly balls put in play against him in 2009, the second-lowest rate among all starters. Expect a few more balls to clear the wall next year. That being said, Greinke is here to stay as a perennial first-round pick. He'll come with a hefty price tag, but he'll be worth it.  (David Golebiewski)</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so3" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The 2009 Cy Young Award winner regressed  in 2010 and disappointed a few people with his 2010 performance. Though he was not great in 2010, Greinke wasn't that bad.   One main change in his 2010 pitching style was that instead of striking everyone out, he was looking to create more ground balls and weak contact. The results show this by his GB% going from 40% to 46% and K/9 dropping from 9.5 to 7.4.  Greinke's plan would not have been half bad if the infield defense playing behind wasn't horrible.  Instead, too many balls got through the infield and his ERA jumped 2.00 points from 2.16 to 4.17.  Giving up the extra runs to go along with the Royals' bad offense led to just 10 wins. Greinke said that he is going to abandon his ground-ball strategy and go back to striking everyone out.  It wouldn't be unexpected to see him put up numbers somewhere between his 2009 and 2010 values.  His trade to the Brewers will probably only help his win total. The Brewers' offense should give him quite a bit more support than the Royals' did. Expect his ERA and WHIP to remain fairly constant since the Brewers' defense is similar to the Royals' (bad), especially since the defensively incompetent Yuniesky Betancourt will probably be manning the shortstop position. Greinke should be in the top-five to -15 pitchers drafted depending on the league.
