Chen was pretty hittable last season, giving up 193 hits over his 185.2 innings pitched, but his improvement with his command kept him in games, and although he gives up his fair share of fly balls, his home run rate remained about league average. It's likely Chen sees his ERA creep up towards his career rates in 2015, and if he continues to pitch to contact, his WHIP might slide to around 1.30. Without much in the way of strikeouts Chen is probably a better option for AL-only leagues, although he could be usable in deeper standard formats.                 <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Wei-Yin Chen's fourth year in the major leagues was arguably his strongest, as he set career bests in ERA (3.34), park and league adjusted ERA (124), WHIP (1.22), and strikeout rate (19.3%). The Taiwanese lefty continued to be particularly durable, as he made 31 starts for the second straight year. The only outing that Chen missed was in June when the Orioles decided to send him to the minor leagues so that he could avoid a matchup with the lefty-mashing Blue Jays -- a move Chen was openly unhappy with. The decision may have been a wise one, as the southpaw not only has a pronounced platoon split in his career, but it was even worse in 2015 when he held fellow lefties to a .576 OPS but was pounded by righties to the tune of an .815 mark. Chen's 4.16 FIP last year suggests he also may have had some help in preventing runs, but it should also be noted that his career 4.14 mark is notably higher than his lifetime 3.72 ERA. On the other hand, he has excellent command and coaxes pop-ups well with his high, riding fastball. As for next year in Miami, Chen may not exactly rack up the strikeouts but should continue to ride stellar control (career 5.8% walk rate) to middle of the rotation production. If the park suppresses his home run rates -- which have been mediocre -- further than projections, he could be a sneaky number three. The 30-year-old is worth a look in all formats, especially those in which he can be benched when facing tough right-handed heavy lineups on the road. (Dylan Higgins)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Chen is by no means a fantasy ace, but he offers the solid control and resulting decent ratios to contribute in most fantasy leagues. The durably lefty can be especially effective when protected from tough matchups and should provide value almost every time out.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Last year, among the 137 pitchers that threw at least 100 innings, he was 93rd. He lost a mile per hour on his fastball while the rest of his pitches got harder. He lost two inches of drop on his slider and nearly an inch on his curve. He tried to throw low in the zone after years of throwing high in the zone. The league hit more homers than it had ever before, save one season in the middle of the steroid era. All of this led to the biggest home run rate Chen has ever seen, in the same season he left Baltimore for Miami. So weird, yet if you knew all those things would happen, and that he would sprain his elbow in July and miss two months, you'd probably have guessed how terrible his season looked. Will the Marlins let him throw high in the zone? Will he get any of that velocity back? Will he find his slider again? Will the pop-ups come back? It probably won't cost you too much to find out. If it doesn't, take a look under the hood. He won't ever be a front-of-the-rotation fantasy piece with that strikeout rate, but he could be an asset.
