Santiago’s biggest issue is his command. Over the past two seasons, his 3.70 walks per nine is the fifth highest rate out of 100 pitchers (min 250 IP). However, up until that point, fielding independent pitching has always told a different story as Santiago has consistently outperformed his FIP. Could Santiago be the type of pitcher who goes against these advanced numbers? Well, he was moved to Minnesota and the answer became a resounding no as his ERA shot up to 5.58 and his career 77% left-on base percentage went down to 70%, helping to eliminate the narrative that he just knew how to pitch out of jams. More interestingly, his strikeout per 9 went down to 5 in Minnesota, which is over 2 Ks less than his traditional numbers. Santiago was never an overwhelming power pitcher, but for somebody who gives up as many home runs as he does, losing two strikeouts per 9 will crush your performance. The key question for Santiago is whether the strikeouts will come back, and if so, can he continue to outperform his FIP the same way he did with the Angels for years.
