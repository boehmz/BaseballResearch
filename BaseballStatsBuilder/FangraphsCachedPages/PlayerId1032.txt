He doesn't hurt himself with walks, but Happ is playing with fire by allowing so many fly balls, especially when pitching in Philadelphia. The southpaw may find that he'll need his curveball more often in 2010, now that his scouting report has circulated throughout the league. Using mostly fastball/change/cutter, it could help if he changed the hitter's eye level a little more often. With Myers gone and the health/effectiveness of Moyer in question, the Phillies will likely lean more heavily on Happ in 2010, so he should see an increase in his innings pitched.                 <tr id="so3" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Happ, a young starter with prior MLB success, was the centerpiece of Houston's Roy Oswalt trade last season. However, just as it did in Philadelphia, Happ's good ERA is not supported by his peripheral numbers. Happ has consistently walked about four batters per nine innings through his career so far. It has taken a combination of a very low BABIP (.273) and an extremely high LOB% (81.4%) to keep Happ's traditional numbers from creeping below average. Although there is a possibility that Happ is better than the average pitcher at suppressing base hits and stranding base runners, there is zero chance that it continues at this level. No pitcher in MLB history has maintained a LOB% that high over 1,000 innings pitched, and 289 innings is hardly enough to establish the BABIP dominance that he has shown. Expect Happ to struggle to win games behind the terrible Astros lineup. He can put up some strikeout numbers, but his ERA should land around 4.20-4.30, making him a marginal fantasy play. (Jack Moore)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Happ looked good in his first go-round with the Astros, but exercise cautions. He was useful in a big league rotation, but nothing close to worthy of a fantasy roster spot. After being traded to the Pirates at the trade deadline, Happ was one of the best pitchers in baseball, putting together an improbable 1.85 ERA and 2.19 FIP over 11 starts. The difference between Seattle Happ and Pittsburgh Happ? Fastball usage. Happ threw his heater -- which sits around 92 mph -- about 10% more while in the National League. Happ also used his slider more, at the expense of his curveball, as well as his changeup, which he barely even used with the Buccos. Happ’s coming out party earned him a three-year deal with his former employer north of the border, who are hoping at least some of his adjustments under Ray Searage stick. Moving back to the American League and to the hitter’s paradise in Toronto will already hurt Happ, but he’s still easily worth a flier late in drafts, and perhaps a couple extra dollars just in case he’s truly figured something out.
