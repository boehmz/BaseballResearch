With emotions down in the dumps in Queens, Harvey came out and blew the doors off the competition with a 95 mph fastball backed up by a wicked slider and a decent curveball. Much was made of his inadequate changeup in the minor leagues, and the coaching staff coaxed him into throwing the pitch more often in his debut, but with a curveball in hand, Harvey probably won't have platoon issues with his repertoire. A show-me change, used less often, might lead to better results in the future. And it's hard to imagine better results, as the 24-year-old struck out more than a quarter of his batters and showed a miniscule ERA and WHIP. His control wasn't -- and probably won't be -- elite, but with whipeout stuff, it won't need to be. Don't point too hard at his .262 batting average on balls in play: Matt Swartz found that BABIP is negatively correlated with strikeout rate, and Harvey will always strike batters out. Fantasy owners can expect bundles of strikeouts, a strong ERA, and maybe an average WHIP in 2013. He's worth an early flier once all the best number ones and twos are off the board, considering that last year's Yu Darvish might be his floor. (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/enosarris">Eno Sarris</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Matt Harvey is the future for the Mets.                 <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The Dark Knight of Gotham was the most exciting thing on the New York baseball scene in 2013 and was on pace to be a legitimate challenger to Clayton Kershaw’s Cy Young march before being shut down and eventually having Tommy John surgery. Harvey throws four above average pitches, and maintains one of the highest average velocities on his fastball in the game. He’s simply one of the best hurlers in the game. Unfortunately, little of this matters as he will not be pitching next season, as he recovers from surgery. There isn't a great reason to worry that he won’t regain form as soon as he regains health, which makes him an interesting proposition in keeper and dynasty leagues. If you have the room, the budget, or are simply headed for a rebuilding year, throwing a few dollars at Harvey sounds like a wise long-term move. He’s essentially the best prospect in the game who is not much more than a year away from a call-up. (Derek Ambrosino)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>After establishing himself as one of the league’s best pitchers over the first two thirds of the season, Matt Harvey joined the esteemed list of Tommy John surgery patients. He will not pitch in 2014, but will be ready for 2015, most likely in prime form. Depending on your team and league make-up, Harvey could be wise investment despite the injury.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Matt Harvey healthy is definitively the bee's knees. Matt Harvey coming off of Tommy John surgery owns a little more uncertainty. We know there's a general <a href="http://www.hardballtimes.com/a-primer-on-tommy-john-surgery-part-one/">failure rate of something slightly better than 20% overall</a> for the first TJ surgery. We also know that command sometimes takes a breather before returning, as the pitcher gets to know his new ligament better. There are things we think we know, too: <a href="http://www.fangraphs.com/blogs/danny-salazar-on-returning-from-surgery-too-soon/">longer return times are better</a>. So it's good news that Harvey took longer to return, and his upside means we'll all be interested in putting him on our teams. This is a man that did everything right in his second year -- struck everyone out, walked nobody, got grounders, showed velocity, and had secondary stuff that broke in every direction. Take a projection that might result after his two years -- probably top-three numbers -- and then shave 20-25% off to represent the risk inherent in his surgery. You're still left with a top-15 pitcher. Are you willing to pay top-15 prices? (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Because the upside is so glorious, Matt Harvey will still get mid-round attention and cost double-digit cash on auction day. It's not even unreasonable to make that sort of investment. But you have to decide for yourself if you can stomach it.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After missing all of 2014, Harvey was understandably inconsistent through July of last season, especially struggling with control at times. It didn’t last. He was his usual dynamite self once August hit. He walked six batters in 56.1 innings from that point though the rest of the regular season. He looks to be following Adam Wainwright’s path of recovery from Tommy John surgery. Wainwright also failed to find his top-notch command for a bit, then returned to himself the next season. Ready for the impressive stuff? Even without being fully on top of his game from the start, Harvey was one of 11 starters to finish with at least a 20% difference between his strikeout and walk percentages. He had the 16th-best swinging strike rate and the eighth-best contact rate on pitches in the zone. Again, this was after missing all of 2014 following Tommy John surgery. Harvey’s fastball velocity was right on par with his 2013 mark, while the time off may have hurt his feel for his changeup as it was faster on average than 2013 by more than a mile per hour. The smaller difference in velocity made both less effective than in 2013, though still good overall. (Adam McFadden)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Harvey was excellent last season coming off of Tommy John surgery and should be even better this year. He’s an easy call as a top five fantasy pitcher who likely won’t quite be drafted as one.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Anderson went on to post a 6.68 ERA with 8.01 strikeouts per nine innings and 1.93 walks per nine. Hitters pounded him to the tune of a .381 batting average on balls in play and a 19.1 % home run per fly ball ratio. Maybe that's bad luck. He's probably easy to hit. So much for having Harvey's stuff... Of course, Harvey only managed a 4.86 ERA. His fastball velocity dropped 1.5 mph, and his strikeout rate fell to 7.38 K/9. He maintained a healthy 2.43 BB/9, but hitters teed off with the help of a .353 BABIP. Again, maybe that's luck. Probably it's making too many hittable pitches. His fastball, change, and slider all allowed an above-average BABIP while his curve remained effective. Even though Harvey feels like he's been on the scene forever, this is just his fifth season. It's not uncommon for high-quality pitchers to have one blip in the road of an otherwise stellar career. It's even more common for high quality pitchers to erode to average talent levels.
