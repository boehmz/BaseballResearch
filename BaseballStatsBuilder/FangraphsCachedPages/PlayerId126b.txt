                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Kendall Graveman got a ton of grounders in the minor leagues, so his sinker is legit. The shape of his change suggests it could be good, and his slider and cutter seem fine. He's never had command or homer issues either. His velocity was above-average. On the other hand, no pitch really stands out as plus, that velocity was in short stints last year, he's a bit old, and he may have just been putting up good minor league results based on his command. Given a sliver of opportunity at the back end of the Athletics rotation, and a nice new home park, he deserves deep league attention. Not much more until we get more major league data on him, though. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Command and fringy stuff will burn a path through the minor leagues.                 <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>It's been pretty clear what Graveman's game is since he started quickly climbing the ladder of Toronto's minor league system in 2013: focus on command, chip in with a few strikeouts, and above all, keep the ball on the ground. The ceiling for such a pitcher is always going to be slightly limited, but the utility of one can often be overlooked. With a heavy sinker that can touch 95 (and features top 20 vertical movement, minimum 110 innings), and a cutter that was the key to his quick rise through the minors, Graveman has the basics to be a backend of the rotation / swingman type for Oakland in 2016. It's his search for a third pitch that will decide where he ends up in the long run, as he's yet to find a true league average offering between his changeup and curveball. He was also victimized by a high home run per fly ball rate in 2015, which is something worth keeping an eye on given the expectations of his sinker heavy makeup. Assuming he's over the oblique injury that knocked him out of the end of the 2015 season, Graveman might have the stuff to surprise in 2016. After all, pitchers have made their living on ground balls and great command in the past, and if his home run rate stabilizes to league average levels or beyond, a solid starter could be in the offing. (Owen Watson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Coming over to the Athletics in the trade that sent Josh Donaldson to the Blue Jays, Graveman slotted straight into the Oakland rotation for over half of the 2015 season before a season-ending oblique injury. At his best, Graveman is a ground ball machine with good command, though his strikeout ceiling is limited by his current lack of a true out pitch. Still, there's upside in the ground balls and pitcher friendly stadium, and 2016 could yield better than expected value if he's able to stay healthy and keep the ball in the park.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Graveman made just 21 starts in 2015, but he stayed healthy and was allowed to go a full 31 outings last year, good for a career-high 186 innings. The result was a 4.11 ERA, which, when adjusted for park and league, came in right around average (102 ERA-). Graveman had a capable of a solid walk rate -- just 6% of batters he faced -- but he did himself no favors with a modest 14% strikeout rate. Considering the Athletics were near the bottom of the list in a number of defensive metrics, that much contact meant a lot of hits allowed for the Alabama native. Graveman threw his sinker more than half the time, and although his control numbers look much better against fellow righties (career 3.5 strikeout-to-walk ratio vs. right-handed batters) than lefties (1.7 K/BB), the results have not been too different (.743 OPS to .740). Additionally, like many A’s pitchers, Graveman struggled much more when away from Oakland last year (.788 OPS allowed) than when he was at home at the spacious Coliseum (.676). Looking ahead, the 26-year-old’s lack of strikeouts not only limits his fantasy ceiling in the first place, but the extra contact allowed simply inflates his ERA and WHIP (career 1.34 mark) too much to make him any sort of fantasy anchor -- especially until he finds himself in front of a better defense. (Dylan Higgins)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Graveman is an impressive ground-ball artist, but his situation exacerbates the downsides of that kind of fantasy profile. The right-hander either needs to substantially improve his ratios or somehow pick up more strikeouts to become a noteworthy fantasy asset, and neither seems likely to happen in the near future.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
