TINSTAAPP applies doubly to Rockies prospects, and Anderson was never really seen as a likely rotation arm. These sorts of players sometimes pan out at sea level, but at Coors? Let's just call it unusual. Anderson had a successful 19-start debut, working to a luck-neutral 3.54 ERA. He throws a four seamer, a sinker, and a cutter, all of which induce big ground ball rates. As is often the case, the sinker was by far his worst-performing fastball, and there's a good case for him to scrap it entirely. Anderson's most volatile pitch was a frequently-thrown changeup. When put in play, hitters managed a .303 average and .530 slugging percentage against it (.438 batting average on balls in play). He also induced a whiff rate over 20%. The pitch was his best strikeout offering even if it was hit hard. Anderson's success came without a true breaking ball - he threw his curve only 23 times. That offers the 27-year-old an obvious path towards improving. He may also benefit from throwing fewer pitches in the strike zone. It's counterintuitive, but sometimes a pitcher can make hitters uncomfortable by seeming to be a little bit erratic. Nothing in his statistics scream regression. However, it would not be surprising if the stuff was less effective this season. (Brad Johnson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Anderson burst onto the scene late last season after proving he can handle Coors Field.
