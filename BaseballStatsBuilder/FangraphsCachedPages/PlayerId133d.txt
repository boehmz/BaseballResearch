                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Fulmer's Rookie of the Year campaign was unexpected, but not necessarily that surprising as the big righty was a well-regarded prospect coming up with the Mets and then traded to Detroit for Yoenis Cespedes. He arrived with a plus fastball and slider, but the development of his changeup is what took him to the next level and drove his award-winning season. He used it at a 7% clip through his first four starts, but it turned into a 20%-usage weapon from May 21st on, propelling him to a 2.58 ERA/1.00 WHIP combo in 139.2 innings. He wasn't trusting the grip so he spent a bullpen getting comfortable with his Vulcan change and it took off (.404 OPS, 29% K% in 101 PA) in those final 22 starts. He also started using his four-seamer more, particularly up in the zone, and saw immediate dividends there, too. There are reasons to project some regression in Fulmer's ERA with this skill set, but there is also reason to believe he adds to his league-average strikeout rate and does it all in 30 or so more innings, pushing 190+ in a full season with the club. (<a href="http://twitter.com/sporer">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>It took a few starts to settle in, but Fulmer found the right grip on his changeup and soared to a Rookie of the Year win. His ERA indicators predict some regression, but that's only if his skills remain static and there are reasons to believe his strikeout rate will improve, mitigating some of the potential backslide. He isn't coming cheaply, but he's not cost-prohibitive, either. A worthy investment for sure.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
