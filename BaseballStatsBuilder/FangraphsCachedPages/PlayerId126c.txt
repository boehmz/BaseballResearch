Coming into the public eye early in the year as it was revealed that he lives in a 1978 Volkswagen Van, Norris started the year in the Blue Jay rotation but struggled with command and was sent to Triple-A only to be subsequently traded to the Tigers in the David Price deal at the deadline. After the season, Norris revealed that he had been diagnosed with thyroid cancer earlier in the season. Norris had the malignant growth in his throat removed and stated that he was now cancer free. Norris is expected to be in the Tiger rotation out of camp, but will have to focus on keeping his walks down if he wants to live up to the massive potential he has. Throughout the minors he posted extremely high strikeout rates as well as huge walk rates, but last season the strikeouts dropped down while the walks remained high -- until he joined the Tiger rotation. In Detroit he posted a 4.7% walk rate over eight starts, giving some hope that he and the Tiger coaching staff and catchers may be able to cure his biggest flaw. (Ben Duronio)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Norris is cancer free heading into his first full year in Detroit and should begin the season in the team's rotation. It was paired with a 1.37 WHIP that suggests he was running a little hot ERA-wise, but the 23% strikeout rate and 2.9 K:BB rate are more important aspects anyway. The eccentric lefty has a four-pitch mix, all of which can miss bats when they are on. The changeup needs to improve to keep righties at bay more consistently (.800 OPS), but he has had success against them at various times in the minors so it's in there. Health has been a career-long challenge for Norris, too, and two more DL stints in 2016 add to a lengthy list. He showed his best velocity in the majors last year on both the fastball (93.1) and slider (87.5), and results followed, especially with the slider. He cleared 150 total innings for the second straight season and can hopefully stay upright enough to log a total north of that in the majors this year. The promising left-hander could take a major step forward in his age-24 season, but he's priced at a level where even if he doesn't, you're not losing much. (<a href="http://twitter.com/sporer">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Injuries have delayed the emergence of the top prospect, but he finished with something to build on in 2016 and the Tigers are hoping he can make the leap and be a strong mid-rotation contributor all season.
