                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Nick Martinez was forced to jump from Double-A to the majors last season, and he struggled to get easy outs, ending the year with a 4.55 ERA in 140+ innings. A relatively well-regarded prospect, he walked 9% of the batters he faced while striking out a mere 12.6%, well below the league average of 20.4%. An extreme fly-ball pitcher in Texas’ hitters haven, Martinez had a little trouble with the home run ball, and could actually see that problem increase next season due to some simple regression. Martinez’s stuff isn’t bad -- he mixes a low-90s fastball with a slider, curve, and change -- but he lacks a pitch that fools hitters on a consistent basis. Until the 24-year-old can figure out which of his offspeed pitches can be a real weapon, he’ll continue to be unimpressive as a big leaguer. Martinez could probably use some time in Triple-A to work on things, but he may not have that luxury. If he’s in the bigs to start the season, stay away. (Zach Sanders)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Martinez struggled in his jump to the majors, and didn’t show much to inspire hope for the future. Ok, that is not fair. He burst onto the scene by posting a 0.35 ERA (and by following that with a 3.25 ERA in May) but he posted those numbers despite pitching terribly. Isolating those first 10 starts, he posted a 2.03 ERA despite a weak 13% strikeout rate and unimpressive 7% walk rate. His .279 batting average on balls in play didn't seem crazy low, but his 23% line drive rate was not great. He really only survived thanks to a 6% home run per fly ball rate and by stranding more than 83% of the baserunners he let on. Over his next (and final) 11 starts, nothing changed, but everything changed. His strikeout rate (14%) barely moved and his walk rate (9%) ticked up. His BABIP settled at a more expected .305. His home run per fly ball skyrocketed (17%) and he stranded far fewer (65%) baserunners. His ERA jumped to 6.25, backed by a 6.07 FIP. If you somehow still own Martinez in a keeper, maybe you can trade him to someone who believes in that first half. Otherwise, there is no reason for you to think about him in a fantasy context at all. (Chad Young)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Nick Martinez posted a 2.03 ERA over his first ten starts, but looked awful and 100% unsustainable doing it.
