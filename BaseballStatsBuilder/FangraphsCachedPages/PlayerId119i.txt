                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Towards the end of August, Jeff Sullivan observed a curious phenomenon regarding the split-finger fastball and those pitchers who throw it with some frequency. By way of illustration, consider the unordered list below, which features the birthplaces of every starter in 2014 to record at least 100 innings and also post a split-finger usage rate above 20%.  • Hyogo, Japan • Osaka, Japan • Tokyo, Japan • Wyandotte, Michigan, USA  Those are the hometowns of Masahiro Tanaka, Hiroki Kuroda, Hisashi Iwakuma, and Matt Shoemaker, respectively. While, culturally speaking, one of these people isn't like the other, all four pitchers feature remarkably similar repertoires -- including merely average fastball velocities and relatively high split-finger usage. What else they share is success: all four prevented runs at an above-average rate in 2014 and produced the fielding-independent numbers to suggest that such run prevention is sustainable. For the three Japanese pitchers this is perhaps less surprising: each was expressly signed out of Japan with the idea (from the club's point of view) that he'd be an asset to a major-league rotation. Shoemaker, meanwhile, was undrafted after his senior year at Eastern Michigan University and signed afterwards with the Angels for merely $10 thousand. Despite the lack of pedigree, the most likely outcome is that he approximates his 2014 numbers in 2015 -- which he'll begin as the Angels' number three starter, it would appear. (Carson Cistulli)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Matt Shoemaker very probably isn't a Japanese person, nonetheless he has a repertoire most commonly found among former NPB pitchers. Despite a lack of pedigree and arm speed, he's very likely also an above-average major-league starter.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>There was a lot to like about Shoemaker's 2014: he struck out batters at an above average clip, had fantastic command, and posted batted-ball outcomes that suggested what we were seeing probably wasn't a total fluke. 2015 was a relative disaster, however, as his peripherals weakened and his home run per fly ball rate ballooned. With a small decline in velocity between the two seasons and a history of minor league home run problems, we can probably blame a lot of his struggles in the latter campaign to simple regression. Great strikeout and walk rates are hard to fake over the course of an 135-inning season, but the results that Shoemaker posted in 2014 might not be truly representative of his actual talent level. Our own Eno Sarris -- when investigating Shoemaker's excellent strikeout and walk rates -- had this to say on the matter: "... he’s had a history of bad home run rates in the minors. Any excitement about his future needs to go more in the Mike Fiers bin than in the Carlos Carrasco one." With that said, his numbers are likely to settle somewhere in between his 2014 and 2015 marks if given more time. As he's likely to get a shot at full-time work in the back end of the Angels' 2016 rotation, he'll hopefully clear up the currently muddy picture regarding what the expectations for his performance should be. (Owen Watson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>After an extremely successful 2014, Shoemaker took a step back in 2015 and was demoted to Triple-A for a spell in August. In reality, he's probably somewhere in between those two seasons, as his peripherals remained strong in 2015 and he was victimized by a very high home run per fly ball rate. He's a risky but potentially valuable starter that might be drafted at below market rate given his struggles last season, likely slotting into the Angels' fourth or fifth rotation spot.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

From the day he made that decision until the end of the season, the righty put together a 2.93 ERA that was backed by sustainable peripherals. He struck out nearly a batter per inning, walked only a bit more than one per nine innings, and had reasonable ball-in-play rates. Is it sustainable to throw two fastballs and a splitter 95% of the time and stay healthy and successful? Nobody's done it for a full season, and the closest were the recent seasons from Masahiro Tanaka, Jake Odorizzi, and Felix Hernandez. Maybe we don't need to worry about health that much, and maybe it's unfair to ding him for missing time due to a line drive to the face, and those 135.1 innings last year were pretty impressive. Shoemaker comes with decent risk, but he doesn't have the price tag of other high-strikeout, low-walk hurlers out there, so there's usually upside associated with his cost. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>A full, healthy season from Shoemaker will be his first, but if it happens, it may come with 200 strikeouts and a strong WHIP. Seems worth a (mid-level) investment.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
