                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>It was a tale of two seasons for Hughes: In seven starts he had an ERA of 5.45, but in 44 appearances as a reliever he had an ERA of 1.40.  Part of this was luck. He had a BABIP of .267 as a reliever versus .317 as a starter. As well, he had a HR/FB of 4% as a reliever versus 12% as a starter. His results were also based partially on performance, including a strikeout rate of 11.4 K/9 from the ‘pen versus 8.00 K/9 in the rotation, as well as a walk rate of 2.3 BB/9 from the ‘pen versus 3.9 BB/9 in the rotation. Some of this difference is expected, as most pitchers perform better out of the bullpen than the rotation, but Hughes’ starter-reliever performance gap was much larger than average. This raises an important question for the Yankees: Is this larger gap just a small-sample-size fluke or does Hughes’ stuff work naturally better out of the ‘pen than the rotation?<br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>At this point, it looks like Hughes will enter spring training in the rotation, although it is easy to see him moving back to the bullpen if he struggles again as a starter. A safe projection would be 20 starts, 115 innings, an ERA of 4.00, a WHIP of 1.30, 110 strikeouts, and seven or so wins. He does have considerable upside based on his amazing minor league numbers, so a strikeout per inning, an ERA in the mid-3.00s and a WHIP in the 1.20s are possible. Even if he does break out like that, the Yankees organization might limit his innings as it did with Joba Chamberlain in 2009. If Hughes ends up pitching from the bullpen, he gets some value as, most likely, first in line for saves if Rivera goes down (since Joba would likely be in the fifth starter’s spot).                 <tr id="so3" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>During his first full season in the rotation, Hughes was good, though far from the brand-new ace that his 18 wins seemingly indicate. When he was on the mound as the pitcher of record, the Yankees' 2004 first-rounder received a whopping 9.6 runs of support per nine innings pitched from Bomber bats. Sure, the Yankees have a high-powered offense, but that was the highest mark in the AL by more than a run. That said, Hughes missed some bats with 7.5 K/9, and he also limited walks. Getting a first-pitch strike against batters 63% of the time (58.8% MLB average), Hughes issued just 2.96 BB/9. While his mid-70s curveball got scorched (-1.2 runs per 100 pitches thrown), Hughes' low-90s heat (+0.88) and upper-80s cutter (+1.02) were quality offerings. He did surrender a lot of fly balls (36.1 GB%, compared to the 44% MLB average), which led to his coughing up nearly 1.3 homers per nine innings pitched. Overall, Hughes' 4.19 ERA lined right up with his peripherals -- his FIP was 4.25. He'll be the Yankees' No. 2 or 3 starter in 2011, depending upon whether the team can bring in another big name after Cliff Lee declined to don pinstripes.  (David Golebiewski)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Hughes' days in the bullpen are over -- he's now firmly entrenched in the Yankees' rotation. In those three starts, he saw his fastball average 89 MPH after being around 92 in the previous season. He had a 13.94 ERA, 2.23 WHIP, 2.6 K/9 and 3.5 BB/9. Not good. He returned to the Yankees in early July with a fastball near 92 MPH, and his stats over the rest of the season were 4.55 ERA, 1.35 WHIP and 6.4 K/9. All were just a little worse than his career averages. For 2012, expect somewhat of a bounce-back season. If projections are used with him, they may need to be adjusted up a bit to take into account the three bad/injury starts at the beginning of the season. The one item I would watch like a hawk is reports of his fastball speed in Spring Training and at the start the season. He might be a useful starting-pitcher-eligible reliever next year, at worst.                 <tr id="so5" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Phil Hughes regressed in 2011 just when the Yankees thought that their former blue-chip prospect was finally emerging after putting up two solid years prior. Still only 25 on draft day in 2012, many owners took the plunge anyway, and were treated to a mediocre option who provided 16 wins, volume, and little else. Hughes' ERA was 4.23, and his 4.35 xFIP didn't point to him being a different pitcher at any point during the year. While he generally puts up a low walk rate (7.5% career) which portends a lower WHIP, he also lacks serious swing and miss material that many had hoped he'd develop as he matured into an older starting pitcher. Now heading into his age-27 season, hope that Hughes' stuff will eventually play up needs to be tempered, if not abandoned. Being a starting pitcher for the Yanks means you are always draftable; wins are almost assured if you can keep the Bronx Bombers in the game. However, with each passing year, Hughes ceiling falls a couple inches. He shouldn't be drafted as an average option with huge upside, but there is something to be said for pitchers who chew innings and provide wins at the back of fantasy rotations. (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/soxczar">Colin Zarzycki</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Hughes is slowly settling in as a back-of-the-rotation fantasy starter as his xFIP continues to be middling and his 8.3% swinging strike rate is a far cry from what the Yankees envisioned as he tore through the minors in 2006 and 2007. Of course, since the Yankees seem enjoy putting up runs, he's always worth a late-round flier, but it's probably time to stop drafting him like he still has blue-chip upside.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Phil Hughes has struggled to stay on the mound in his career. Since his 2007 debut, Hughes has exceeded 150 innings just twice and never thrown for 200 innings in a season. Despite his up-and-down health, Hughes has remained very consistent statistically. In his three (relatively) full seasons, Hughes has struck out between 7.45 and 7.76 batters and walked between 2.16 and 2.96 walks per nine each season. Apart from health, the major obstacle for Hughes has been the long ball, and given that his 33% groundball rate is the fourth-lowest among qualified starters since 2010, he could not have been a worse fit for Yankee Stadium. In his career, Hughes has allowed nearly twice as many home runs at home (1.69 home runs per nine) than on the road (0.86 home runs per nine). Enter Target Field. Where Yankee Stadium had a 110 home run factor in 2013, Hughes' new home park (he signed a three-year deal with the Twins) had a 93 home run factor. Hughes remains a major health risk, but when he does pitch, expect an ERA closer to his 4.10 career road ERA than his 4.96 career home ERA. (Scott Spratt)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Phil Hughes received a major upgrade in ballpark in moving from the Yankees to the Twins. That should help him cut down on his home runs allowed and approach an ERA in the threes. Unfortunately, health remains the major concern with Hughes.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>When Hughes hit free agency, it was hard to imagine a better fit than Minnesota. Even coming off a rough season in the Bronx, the Twins needed him far more than he needed them. Target Field was perfectly suited to his skillset; left-handed hitters are effectively neutralized and the park plays very big, which is extremely accommodating to a flyball guy such as Hughes. So while Hughes having a solid season with the Twins wasn't such a big surprise, the lengths to which he took it certainly qualify as such. Hughes set the all-time strikeouts-to-walks ratio at 11.6, as the right-hander pounded the strike zone with far more frequency than his contemporaries. Hughes famously missed a contract incentive calling for an extra $500k to be paid out had he simply recorded one more out, but he shook off the Twins' overtures to get him an extra appearance in relief over the final weekend, simply stating his season was "done." And what a season it was. Even if it isn't the real Phil Hughes, it appears to be a guy who was in need of a classic change of scenery. Worth watching: Hughes actually pitched better on the road (2.78 ERA) than at home (4.25). So while there is room for regression on other fronts, he can still show improvement at Target Field moving into the future. (Brandon Warne)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Here's a fun tidbit: Phil Hughes had 93 strikeouts and eight walks on the road in 2014. He also had 93 strikeouts and eight walks at home. As long as Hughes stays healthy, he seems like a good bet to retain solid fantasy value moving forward even with some regression in that walk rate. The Twins outfield is only going to get better as Byron Buxton and Eddie Rosario ascend on Minneapolis.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Hughes inevitably took a step back from his historic 2014 in which he broke the all-time walk rate record for a single season. And while Hughes continued to pound the strike zone as he had in 2014 -- no pitcher over 150 innings threw to a higher zone percentage than he did -- the zone pounded back, as opposing batters hit the right-hander to the tune of a .293/.311/.501 line. That's the sort of thing you'd expect Yoenis Cespedes (career .271/.319/.486 hitter) to put up. Hughes was clearly working through some health issues at various points in the season, as back and other maladies limited him to just 25 starts after making 32 the year before. He also saw a precipitous decline in his strikeout rate, as it tumbled from 8.0 in 2014 to just 5.4 last season. Chances are the truth remains in the middle, and that is a pitcher who should give the Twins about 180 innings of league-average pitching with a ~3.80 ERA, no walks and a few too many home runs. That has value, especially to a team that willingly employed Mike Pelfrey the last three seasons. (Brandon Warne)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Hughes will come at a cheap price on draft day and could end up turning in some value. It does take a leap of faith however. At least he pitches in a park that is good for his skillset and he won't turn 30 until midseason, so he may be worth a spin.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Realmuto ended his season. That originally wasn't expected to end Hughes' season, but rather cost him 6-to-8 weeks from the June 9 incident as he was diagnosed with a compression fracture above his left knee. Less than one month later, Hughes underwent successful surgery -- except it was to remove a rib to alleviate thoracic outlet syndrome. Hughes reported on Twitter in mid-January that he was throwing a bullpen, but it's still unclear if he'll be ready to start the season, though the widespread expectation is that he will be. Assuming he is, he's coming off posting a 5.95 ERA in 12 appearances (11 starts). Hughes had pitched poorly enough to be moved to the bullpen in June — he was making his first relief appearance when he got hurt against the Marlins — but despite all of that, he will probably get another crack in the rotation, as he's owed $39.6 million over the next three seasons. It's fair to wonder if the issues addressed by the surgery plagued him in a subpar 2015 season — especially with how good he was in his first year in Minnesota in 2014 — but it's going to be very difficult to trust him at all in fantasy after his last two seasons of injuries and performance. If he can get anywhere close to his 2014 performance, he'll be a diamond in the rough. Otherwise, he might just be a plug-and-play option at best.
