                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Despite possessing both (a) slightly above-average velocity for a starter and (b) minor-league strikeout and walk rates of 23.4% and 5.8%, respectively -- which is to say, very strong minor-league strikeout and walk rates -- Montero has never been considered an elite prospect, only appearing once, for example, among Baseball America's top-100 list. In part, the lack of enthusiasm regarding Montero is likely a product of his height. (He's listed at six feet tall.) Scouting orthodoxy suggests that shorter pitchers throw on a flatter plane than their taller counterparts, and are more likely to concede hard contact as a result. In the case of Montero, scouting orthodoxy might be correct. In his first exposure to major-league hitters -- 44.1 innings in 2014, mostly as a starter -- Montero allowed 1.62 home runs per nine innings. A product, that, both of a lower-than-average ground-ball rate (33.9%) and higher-than-average rate of homers per fly ball (14.5%). Of course, one notes that both trends were reversed in his 10 innings this past year. Unfortunate, that detail, for the presiding narrative. In either case, after an injury-shortened 2015 campaign, Montero is expected to enter 2016 healthy. He won't start in the rotation, but is a real candidate to get innings in a relief capacity. (Carson Cistulli)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Montero's relatively diminutive stature either is or isn't responsible for his elevated home run rates in the minors.
