                <tr id="so5" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>A highly-desirable college draft pick in 2012, Wacha dominated out of the bullpen after turning pro, in an effort to keep his innings total down. The right-hander has a very impressive pitching frame and injuries have not been a concern so he should be quite durable.                 <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After Michael Wacha's dominant rookie performance in both the 2013 regular and post-season, the issues around his fantasy value are not what leagues he should be drafted in (all, outside of bizarro leagues), but instead what tier he is in. His 2013 rate stats placed him among the top pitchers in baseball. His control looked just above average, but his ability to strike opposing batters was elite. Wacha's repertoire is effective against both lefties and righties due to his plus-plus change up. Being just 23 at the start of the 2014 season means that Wacha is still further away from a gradual decline. The only real concerns with Wacha are both related to his his limited 2013 workload. Between the majors and the minors, Wacha pitched just around 150 innings in 2013. That is not a low number, but the Cardinals might want to be careful to make sure he does not wear down as Shelby Miller did. The other issue is sample size: yes, Wacha dominated the majors, but it was still just over 64 innings (and only nine starts). His .275 batting average on balls in play is not extremely low, but not many pitchers are true-talent .275 BABIP hurlers, and his strand rate was also high. This is not to say that Wacha is bad, or even just average. He is better than that, and in keeper leagues especially, his youth makes him a very good pick. But in relation to 2014 performance, it may be premature to draft him as a fantasy number one. His upside has a good chance to make that look silly in retrospect, just be wary. (Matt Klaassen)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Michael Wacha had an amazing rookie debut in 2013. If he is that good again in 2014, he will be one of the to pitchers in both fantasy and real baseball. As good as he is, though, drafting him as a number two makes more sense for now, although he has more value in keeper leagues due to his relative youth.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Wacha's fastball/changeup combo made him a revelation in 2013. He joined the Cardinals' rotation in early September, and remained razor-sharp through five regular-season starts and five post-season starts. Last year, the 23-year-old suffered from a stress reaction in his right shoulder -- also known as Brandon McCarthy Syndrome. Wacha was limited to just 107 innings and pitched quite well, but his strikeout rate took a major hit, dropping from 9.05 per nine down to a 7.91 K/9. Wacha is expected to be at 100% health heading into 2015, but it remains to be seen whether his shoulder problems are a long-term concern, or just a short-term detour. For fantasy purposes, his upside is well worth the risk. If he stays healthy, his combination of pure stuff and advanced control will make him a valuable commodity in all leagues. (<a onclick="return false;" target="blank" href="https://twitter.com/scottstrandberg">Scott Strandberg</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>The key to Wacha's 2015 is clearly his health. If he can stay off the disabled list, his floor is likely a top-50 fantasy starter, with a ceiling far higher. It's usually best to shy away from drafting recently injured players in re-draft leagues, but the fact that he came back and got some innings in at the end of 2014 is reassuring, and his upside is still very high.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>After deciding to forgo surgery late in 2014 when a potentially serious shoulder problem popped up, Wacha came back strong in 2015, showing increased velocity alongside the consistent strikeout and walk rates he posted during his 19 starts the previous year. Though his home run per fly ball rate increased from years past to settle around league average -- taking his ERA a notch closer to where xFIP tells us it perhaps should be -- his penchant for inducing soft contact kept his batting average on balls in play below average, where it's been for his entire career. He's nothing if not steady, as his two starting campaigns have shown almost identical strikeout and walk rates. The main concern going forward is fatigue and health, as Wacha's September of 2015 showed signs that his mechanics were slightly altered. Whether that was an intentional change is unknown, but the results weren't pretty: a reduced strikeout rate (17%), exploded walk rate (16.1%), and tons of hard contact (38.4%, seventh-worst among starters with 20+ innings) made the month one to forget. The hope is that Wacha can keep increasing his workload in 2016, taking his good health and consistency from start to finish. Whether he will achieve that goal is unknown, but he possesses all of the tools to do so, and he will be counted on as a solid starting option for the Cardinals this season. (Owen Watson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>In many ways, Wacha's 2015 was a big success: he stepped into a full-time starting role, stayed healthy after worrying shoulder problems surfaced in 2014, and posted solid peripherals and good overall numbers. His September was an absolute disaster, however, and the causes -- whether they were mechanical, injury, or fatigue related -- are unknown. 2015's performance was legitimate, but Wacha has yet to prove he can pitch effectively over the entire course of a season.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

He managed to pitch a mostly full season in 2015 with a solid ERA and decent peripherals, but he was pretty much shot by the time the playoffs came around. Last year, he couldn't make it that far. Wacha was decent in the first half last year, putting up average walk and strikeout numbers, though some poor luck due to a low left-on-base percentage gave him a below-average 4.36 ERA. He only made it a month into the second half before he hit the disabled list with shoulder inflammation. He made a few outings near the end of the season, but did not pitch well. Heading into the 2017 season, there is a possibility that Wacha will not make the Opening Day rotation as he appears to have been supplanted by Alex Reyes. He could have a super-bullpen role or he might find himself as an occasional starter due to injury somewhere else in the rotation. Wacha hasn't shown he can hold up for a full season since his injury in 2014, and expectations should align with that performance.
