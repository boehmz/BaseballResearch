                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Soft contact wasn’t something Wisler was able to induce much of in 2015 -- only 16.8% of the time was he able to stay off of the barrel. One thing that helped him was his ability to induce pop ups. His 14.0% infield fly ball rate had him tied for seventh amongst pitchers who tossed 100+ innings. IFFB% is generally based on a small sample and tends to fluctuate from year to year, so if that number drops and he isn’t the beneficiary of those pop ups, he’ll need to boost his strikeout rate to compensate for those easy outs. Steamer has him increasing his K% and dropping his walk rate, culminating in a K%-BB% in double digits. Wisler features a four-seamer and sinker, complemented by a slider, change, and a curve he throws a few times a game. The curve is something that could likely be scrapped. He gave up liners 40% of the time with it, and surrendered a .364 batting average on the pitch too. One thing Wisler needs to work on is grooving pitches. He threw almost 6% of his pitches right down the heart of the plate. He tries to live in the four and seven keys against lefties, and in the nine key against righties. He needs to work on keeping his misses down and away to righties and lefties, not letting himself miss right down the middle. Lots to work on for the young right-hander, but lots of room for growth. He’s not going to get you many wins this year, and won’t be a huge help in ratios. The upside -- based on velocity and pedigree -- is there if he can figure some things out, so don’t be shy about taking him late if you need an arm. (Darren Schienbein)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Yet another Braves starter who has issues with left-handed batters. It’s almost as if that’s a theme. Wisler isn’t someone that you need to go out of your way to pick up, and if you really want him, he’ll be available to you in the late rounds of your draft. There is definite room for improvement, and if he figures a few things out in 2016, he could be worth the investment.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Matt Wisler made some incremental improvements after a lackluster 2015, but they weren't quite good enough to keep him in the Braves rotation for the entire season. Wisler nudged his strikeout rate up from 15.1% to 17.1% (still below the league average of 20.2%), and dropped his walk rate a touch from 8.4% to 7.3%. Home runs were a major issue for Wisler in 2015, thanks in large part to a 43% fly ball rate; he dropped this to 38% last year, though more of his fly balls turned into homers last year, meaning that his actual home run rate stayed about the same. He improved significantly against left-handed hitters, but largely offset this by pitching worse against righties. All this added up to an ERA right at 5.00 (with matching estimators) and a late-season stint in AAA, though he did return to the majors for the end of the year. Wisler is still young and improving, but with R.A. Dickey and Bartolo Colon newly-signed by Atlanta, there's no guarantee he'll start 2017 in the Braves rotation. Even if he does, he probably has league-average upside at best and is more likely to be an occasional spot starter in fantasy. (Brice Russ)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Wisler took some small steps towards being a league-average pitcher last year, but it probably wasn't enough to guarantee him a slot in the Braves rotation in 2017.
