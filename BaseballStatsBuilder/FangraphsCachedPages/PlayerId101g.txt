His fastball sat firmly at 93 mph at the MLB level and he mixed in three other offerings including a slider, curveball and change up. No surprisingly, the young hurler struggled with his fastball command at times but his change up was a nasty offering for him. Perez, 22, will enter 2014 as the Rangers' fourth or fifth starter, assuming the club doesn't add another established arm. With his 43.1 minor league innings included, he pitched almost 180 combined innings in 2013 so 200 frames might be attainable in the coming season.                 <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Coming into the 2012 season, Perez was a top-50 prospect on the brink of his debut. Four years later, that prospect sheen has worn off. Those years were plagued with injuries and poor performance; any hope he would become a fantasy stud is probably gone. He did post a 3.40 FIP in 78.2 innings last year, but there are some real concerns. First, that FIP was helped by a 5% home run per fly ball rate, well below his career number (9%) and not likely sustainable, particularly in homer-happy Arlington. He is an extreme ground ball pitcher, which will help keep the ball in the park, but Perez allows an awful lot of balls in play -- he strikes out only 14% and walks 7% of batters. Out of 190 pitchers with 70+ innings in 2015, only 27 allowed batters to put the ball in play (or over the fence) more often than Perez's 79%. He should see some batted ball rate improvement and an uptick in left on base rate, but as his 4.46 ERA moves towards that FIP, his FIP will move up with increased home runs. The net will be an ERA right around 4.00, with almost no strikeouts (5.49 K/9 last year, 5.91 for his career). (Chad Young)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Don't let the 3.40 FIP fool you -- Martin Perez is, at best, a decent ERA with too few strikeouts to matter. More likely, the ERA is a bit worse than decent.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

The follow up was cut short after just eight starts and a Tommy John surgery that essentially cost him the next two seasons (130 innings in 2014-15). The 2016 season saw his already-weak strikeout rate drop further (12%) while his walk rate climbed to 9% and his home run rate ticked back toward the 2013 level (0.8) en route to a 4.39 ERA/4.50 FIP combo. He’s simply not good enough against righties, and the quickest way to fixing that might be trusting the high fastball more. Since 2014, his 16% of fastballs in the upper third of the zone to righties is lowest among 54 lefties with at least 1000 fastballs thrown. Yet his .485 OPS on the 90 plate appearances ending on those pitches is sixth-best, though it’s weak contact all the way as his 10% strikeout rate is third-worst and painfully below the 26% average. Scrapping the curveball against them would also help (career .949 OPS, albeit in just 115 PA), though he seems set on <a href="http://www.star-telegram.com/sports/spt-columns-blogs/gil-lebreton/article133772629.html">revamping it</a> instead. He hasn’t even reached 500 MLB innings yet so there is some time, but it’s quickly running out. (<a href="http://twitter.com/sporer">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Perez ate up nearly 200 innings with a league-average performance after being limited to just 130 innings in 2015-16 thanks to Tommy John surgery. The 26-year old lefty was lauded as a prospect, but has failed to pay off on the potential. There are some intriguing aspects, but he needs to make sharp improvements — and fast — if the prospect hype is to be met.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
