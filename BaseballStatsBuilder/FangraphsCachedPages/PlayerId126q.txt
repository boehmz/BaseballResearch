                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Andriese split the 2015 season between the majors and the minors, and between the rotation and the bullpen when he was in the majors. His minor league numbers (2.35 ERA and 1.94 FIP) suggested he had reached new level of development. But his major league numbers (4.11 ERA and 4.14 FIP) suggested he could still use some more seasoning in the minors. He managed to create more strikeouts during his time as a reliever, but the Rays still like him as a sixth or seventh man in the rotation. In fact, during many of his relief appearances, he was asked to pitch two or more innings as a part of Kevin Cash's double-starter technique. Even if the Rays rotation stays healthy in 2016, Andriese could still find himself in the majors as the Rays attempt to do the double-starter thing again -- limiting starters to two-times through the order, and then having a second starter take the game to the late-game relievers. That requires a good deal of roster manipulation to keep fresh arms in the bullpen, and that could mean good things for Andriese's major league hopes. (<a href="https://twitter.com/BradleyWoodrum">@BradleyWoodrum</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>He's the odd man out of the Rays rotation right now, but Andriese could see playing time -- even without an injury to a Rays starter -- just because of manager Kevin Cash's aggressive bullpen tactics. Andriese should perform well from the 'pen, and he should blossom into a decent back-end starter someday soon.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Andriese is not only slow to the plate, but he had a problem with balks in the minors while working through his issues throwing to first. He is not Jon Lester in that regard, but it is a problem for him, and the opposition knows it. Andriese tied with Taijuan Walker for the highest opponents' stolen-base percentage at 94%, and only Gerrit Cole had more steals against him in fewer innings worked. When not struggling to slow the running game down, he boasts a slightly better-than-average strikeout rate for starting pitchers and is stingy with walks around issues with homers. He is more intriguing than your typical fifth starter because of the cut-change he throws, and while it isn't Kyle Hendricks quality, it is <a href="http://theprocessreport.net/andriese-and-the-cut-up/" target="_blank">good enough to confuse</a> both opposing hitters and even his battery mate.<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Andriese should have the leg up on the fifth spot in the rotation, but he could also serve as the long man in the pen.
