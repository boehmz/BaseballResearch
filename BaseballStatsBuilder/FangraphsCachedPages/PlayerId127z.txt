And for a number of seasons as a minor leaguer, Cole's performances complemented his pedigree well, as he paired excellent velocity with excellent strikeout and walk rates. The numbers were less impressive in 2014-15, however, and so was the arm speed. Consider: in three appearances for the Nats this past season -- his first three as a major leaguer -- Cole produced an average fastball velocity of just 90.6 mph according to PITCHf/x. Considerably lower, that, than the 94-97 mph at which he sat when FanGraphs writer Nathaniel Stoltz saw him in July of 2013. That 2013 season is also the last in which Cole recorded a strikeout rate above 20% at any level where he spent a substantial amount of time. Given the resume and relative youth (he's just 24), Cole can hardly be considered a failure. He enters the 2016 season without a role in the majors, however, and will have to demonstrate some manner of positive regression to worry major league batters. (Carson Cistulli)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Once a formidable pitching prospect, Cole has preserved neither the velocity nor strikeout rates from his best years in the minors. He'll start the 2016 season at Triple-A, most likely.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

The young right-hander has already spent two and a half years at Triple-A so a return to the minors may do more harm than good as he’s in danger of stagnating. He has a four-pitch mix with an average fastball and solid control, which could allow him to develop into a reliable fourth starter but it might make more sense for both the club and Cole to allow him to break camp as a long reliever in the majors. An injury to any of the club’s starters could also provide the rookie hurler with a spot in the rotation so you have to expect Cole to make some meaningful starts for the Nationals at some point in 2017 - although Austin Voth and Erick Fedde are other pitchers that could challenge Cole for opportunities. If he does open the year in the minors, he’ll need to work on his changeup to help him neutralize left-handed batters (.304 vs LHB vs .236 vs RHB in ’16). (Marc Hulet)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Cole doesn’t have the stuff to be an impact pitcher at the big league level but he could be a solid innings-eater type after pitching 110 or more innings each of the past five seasons.
