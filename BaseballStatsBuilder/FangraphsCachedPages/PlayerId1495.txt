                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>The 21-year-old finished with a .500 record, but Kershaw was dominant in virtually every other category.  His fastball averaged nearly 94 mph and trailed only Chris Carpenter’s with a 1.48 wFB/C.  His curveball was even more effective, with a 1.54 wCB/C.  And if that was not enough, in June he added a slider to his repertoire.  Not merely a different look for hitters, Kershaw’s slider is another outstanding offering, with a 1.36 wSL/C.  By the end of the season, Kershaw was throwing his slider (16%) more often than his curve (11%).  It all gave him plenty of weapons to attack hitters, and resulted in a 9.64 K/9, the sixth-best mark in the Majors among starters.  After being an extreme ground-ball pitcher in 2008 (1.54 GB/FB), Kershaw allowed more flies than grounders last year.  But he tied Carpenter for the fewest homers allowed (seven) among qualified starters.<br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>Due in part to his 4.79 BB/9 rate, Kershaw’s FIP (3.08) was higher than his ERA (2.79) last year.  Add in that he had a 77.4 LOB% and his HR/FB rate was an unsustainable 4.1% and Kershaw will likely see his ERA rise.  But there are so many variables to consider, it makes projecting him a difficult task.  Is he a ground-ball or fly-ball pitcher?  Can he continue to throw nasty pitches with both a curve and slider?   Will Kershaw approach the 2.79 BB/9 rate he had at Double-A in 2008?  But while his ERA may be up in the air, it is likely that Kershaw will top last year’s victory total.  The other nine starters in 2009 to top 9.0 K/9 averaged 14.6 W last year.  The Dodgers club was very careful with Kershaw’s work load, as he threw 171 innings in 31 starts.  Even with a minimal increase in innings pitched, Kershaw should approach 200 strikeouts next year.                 <tr id="so3" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Kershaw has great stuff and has been a highly thought of prospect since his days in high school, so after posting  a 2.91 ERA as a 22-year-old, it’s easy to buy into the narrative that he is one of the game’s elite pitchers.  I wouldn’t make that leap just yet – a bulk of Kershaw’s run prevention were built on the twin towers of unsustainability – HR/FB rate (just 5.8%) and BABIP (.275).  While Kershaw may prove to be able to beat the league average in both of the marks, smart money will bet on significant regression in both areas in 2011, and without a drastic improvement in his command, his ERA will almost certainly go up this year.   Consistency in strike throwing is still a problem as well, and high pitch counts have led to shortened outings and fewer opportunities for wins.  While he has all the potential to become an ace, he isn’t there yet, and if prices reflect a belief that he arrived as a #1 starter in 2010, your money is better spent elsewhere.  (Dave Cameron)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>A very good pitcher who might be unfairly perceived as a great one already.                 <tr id="so5" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Kershaw's 2012 was pretty darn close to his 2011, and if not for a spectacular season (and story) from R.A. Dickey, Kershaw might have repeated as the NL's Cy Young award winner. Kershaw did slip a little in 2012. His strikeout rate dropped from 9.57 to 9.05 and his walk rate rose to 2.49 from 2.08. But his K/9 was still the fifth-highest in the National League. His BB/9 was only good for 23rd-best, and contributed to a 1.02 WHIP, compared to 0.98 in 2011. But Kershaw continued to excel at the things that make him an elite pitcher. He induced swinging strikes on 11% of his pitches and held hitters to a .204 batting average. Only Gio Gonzalez had a lower BAA in the National League. Kershaw's kept his batting average on balls in play at or below .275 for the fourth consecutive season, which suggests Kershaw's pitches induce more weakly-hit balls. That's especially true in 2012, when the Dodgers' defense rated only 8th-best in the National League. If Kershaw stays healthy -- not a given, considering his hip issues in the past -- expect another monster season from the lanky left-hander in 2013. He misses bats on more than 10% of his pitches, and induces weak contact when hitters do connect. Look for Kershaw to dominate the National League again in 2013.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Never draft a pitcher in the first round, unless his last name is Kershaw. He is the ultimate fantasy pitcher in that both his health and his skills are incredibly stable and he excels across the board statistically. The only variance in his game is his win total, proving he is at least human and not immune to issues other pitchers have to deal with. Over the past five seasons, his strikeout rate has been at least 25% and over the last three seasons, he has put the struggles with control behind him.Combine the low walk totals with the very low home run totals and low hit rates, and you have the perfect formula for fantastic ratios. The surprising thing is that he is getting even better. His swing and miss rate has improved each of the past five seasons, and that hammer of God curve he throws has kept his chase rate over 30% each of the past three seasons. He is a perfect example of pitcher establishing their own batting average on balls in play baseline as his 5-year average is .270; he scoffs at your .290-.310 range. There is no reason to expect anything different in 2014. (Jason Collette)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>The old adage goes that you should never, ever, draft a pitcher in the 1st round. Rules are made to be broken, and you should do so with Kershaw in 2014.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

It would surprise nobody if Kershaw leads the league, or is close to it, in all fantasy baseball categories and that places him firmly number one on all draft boards among starting pitchers. A few years ago, the idea of any starting pitcher having an ERA under 2 sounded insane, Kershaw could do it for the third year in a row. Wow. That he allowed an uncharacteristic 10% home run per fly ball rate, his highest since his 2008 debut, may have cost him the trophy. But who cares? His strikeout rate has now remained above 30% for two straight seasons, and his control, which at one time was his bugaboo, is now elite. He has even pushed his ground ball rate up to the 50% range. This man is definitely a robot sent from the future to make a mockery of this wonderful game we call baseball. His 15.6 K/BB ratio is the best ever with a minimum of 140 innings, but it takes 162 to qualify for a single-season record. The back injury undoubtedly adds risk to Kershaw’s profile for the first time in about six seasons. Previous injury is the strongest indicator of future injury so it can’t be ignored, and yet his numbers are still so incredibly elite that his draft stock barely takes a hit.
