                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Put in your eyeballs, nerds, and watch this pitcher actually pitch for a bit, and you'll know why he was atop prospect lists on the way up. He's got 97 mph filth plus a hard splitter that leaves hitters scratching their heads. He adds impeccable control and college-arm polish. He'll be 23 next year and not ready to start losing velocity just yet. Yeah, the slider isn't a great offering, and maybe the sinker needs work -- neither pitch got average whiffs or grounders -- but even as he was, 22 and in the bigs for the first time after less than 100 innings in the minor leagues, he managed to strike out more than a batter per inning and show a great walk rate. It's just all the balls in play that didn't work out. Maybe a 25% line drive rate and nearly one in five fly balls turning into home runs is really a thing to worry about. But most research says those things are not stable year to year and need much more sample to be believable. So believe the stuff! Believe the command! Believe the pedigree! Believe the filth. Believe in Kevin Gausman as one of the best young sleeper starters in fantasy baseball in 2014, considering his debut and uncertain place on the depth chart can't make him an expensive acquisition. (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/">Eno Sarris</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>As the only starting pitcher on the Orioles with ace-like upside, Kevin Gausman has to be considered a relative lock for the starting rotation in 2014. If he gets the job, he immediately becomes one of the best sleeper starting pitchers in baseball -- his stuff is so nasty, his division doesn't matter.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>The pieces are there for greatness. A 95 mph fastball. A history of good command. A diving, fading splitter of death. Kevin Gausman has not yet, however, been great. The first problem is that he hasn't been able to show a good breaking pitch. An 8% whiff rate on his slider is well below the 13% average, and he gives up more homers per pitch on the slider than any other pitch he throws. A .225 isolated slugging percentage allowed... very little horizontal movement... very little velocity (82 mph)... the slider's probably not going to be any good as it is. Another problem is that his team won't let him learn a cutter, which might his best option going forward. There's a chance that he could just ditch the slider altogether and be the rare fastball/change/splitter pitcher, though. His regular change is flatter and harder, and if he figured out how to use two fastballs and two changeups correctly, he could still be great. Perhaps more high fastballs could do the trick. The problem is that his overall swinging strike rate and standard peripherals were underwhelming last year, and you may end up having to bid against others that fell in love with his velocity. If you can pay sleeper prices, go ahead. The pieces are there. But if you have to pay as if this puzzle has been put together, maybe it's best to pass. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>If you can pay prices that assume that Kevin Gausman is a puzzle that is not yet put together, go ahead and do so. Those puzzle pieces are intriguing. But if you're asked to pay the price for a completed puzzle, this pitcher is a few hundred pieces short of being a known quantity.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Call me crazy, but it seems like Baltimore's front office doesn't even care about our fantasy teams. Seems pretty rude if you ask me, but get this: they also didn't ask me. Gausman hasn't been able to lock down a steady role in his three seasons with the Orioles. He started all 20 of his appearances in 2014, but he was slingshotting to and from Triple-A with unsettling regularity which is why he only made 20 starts in the first place. In 2013 and 2015, he's worn out the path to Norfolkand and back. His debut saw only a taste of the rotation (five starts) while this year it was just a taste of relieving at the outset (eight appearances), but it's hard to get a read on Gausman's performance with this kind of constant tumult. There is still plenty to like, though: he made strikeout, walk, swinging strike, and ground ball improvements this year. He has an excellent 95-96 mph fastball that yielded 21% strikeout rate, 18th among the 95 starters with 1200 thrown. He is one of only 14 pitchers relying heavily on a splitter (minimum 300 thrown) and his is a big strikeout pitch that is particularly adept at stifling lefties. The slider (or curve) needs work and could be the missing ingredient for a huge season. If the Orioles can supply the stability of role, Gausman seems ready to deliver a breakthrough season. Pay for the Steamer projection knowing there is good bit of upside. Buy. (<a href="http://thespguide.com/">Paul Sporer</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Gausman started the season in the pen, got hurt, came back as a starter, and then was jerked between Norfolk and Baltimore for a month until finally settling in the rotation from late July on. Volatility of role and even team bled onto the field with uneven results, but the 25-year old righty still holds a great deal of intrigue.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so9" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Gausman finished the 2016 season with a career high in innings (179.2), nearly one strikeout per nine innings pitched (8.7 K/9), and his highest percentage of swings induced outside the strike zone (35%) since his 2013 debut, but most fantasy owners will base any 2017 optimism upon 76 of his innings between August and October that helped propel him to a 3.10 second half earned run average. What fueled the second half improvement? More than anything else, it looks like a higher usage of his split changeup, which was always hailed as Gausman’s bread and butter pitch when coming up through the minor leagues. Gausman not only increased his split usage year over year, but his reliance upon the pitch increased as the season went on, spiking in September. Throwing a great pitch more often seems like an obvious step in the right direction for Gausman heading into 2017, but even with a development jump it appears his potential for true breakout will still be tied to his ability to suppress the home run (at least 1.36 home runs per nine innings in each of the last two seasons). Strangely, his home run issue has been much more of a problem away from Camden Yards, so keep an eye on his home/road splits as he takes the mound in 2017. Gausman will pitch all of 2017 at the age of 26 which, combined with small but tangible improvements in pitch usage should imply at least the possibility of a larger step forward coming soon. No one will blame you for reaching or spending just a bit more for Gausman based on those factors alone, but just remember these things don’t always work out the way they’re supposed to, especially with Baltimore starters. (Trey Baughn)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Gausman’s 2016 doesn’t look like the anticipated breakout his pedigree had us all expecting, but it does look like a player making small but necessary adjustments to remain above average across the board. Don’t shy away from Gausman’s youth or unrealized potential heading in 2017, and don’t be surprised if his positive steps forward continue, even if they come with more bumps along the way.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
