                <tr id="so2" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Year in Review: </b>Gordon turned in a 2009 season that he would rather forget. The former Golden Spikes award winner was looking to build upon a 2008 campaign in which he raised his walk rate and ISO. Instead, he did his best Tony Pena Jr. impression at the plate before undergoing hip surgery in mid-April. Gordon returned to action in July, only to be optioned to Triple-A in August after another tepid offensive performance. Happily, the 26-year-old third baseman caught fire in September and early October, raising his season line to a still-gruesome .232/.324/.378. On the positive side, Gordon did continue to work the count well. However, his ISO dipped to .146, and he hit a ground ball nearly 44% of the time. (His career rate is 36%). Chopping the ball into the dirt more often is not a good strategy for a guy with a bum hip. <br /><br /><b>The Year Ahead: </b>It's probably best not to infer too much from Gordon's injury-shortened 2009 season, but his career is clearly at a crossroads. He terrorized minor league pitching, yet he has authored a mild .250/.331/.415 line in the Majors. Gordon has been neutralized by left-handed pitching, with a paltry career .653 OPS versus southpaws. While he has hit fastballs well in all three seasons, he has scuffled against breaking pitches, with run values well below the Major League average. Scouts envisioned Gordon becoming a patient, powerful force at the dish. To this point, though, he has been a platoon hitter who can mash fastballs but looks lost when a pitcher throws him something that dips or darts. Fantasy owners do at least get the chance to buy low on Gordon entering the 2010 season. He still has a chance to be a quality player, but those predictions of stardom look bleak.                 <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Alex Gordon has turned into a unexciting player who gives his owners a fairly consistent return. He's past any hitting peak and it is all downhill here from now, but most of us can say that for ourselves -- his boring production and decline shouldn't scare owners away. He projects to hit around .275 with 15 to 22 home runs and 10 stolen bases. The problem I have with Gordon is a move the Royals made at the end of May when they replaced hitting coaches. Before that move, Gordon was hitting .329/.371/.486. That dropped to .233/.306/.388 after the move, which might not be worrisome if there weren't reasons for the drop. He went from hitting 33% fly balls in the season's first half -- something he'd done to produce despite Kauffman Stadium's size -- to 47% fly balls in the second half. The fly ball increase is the main reason his batting average on balls in play dropped from .347 to .265. And yet it didn't help his power -- the increase in fly balls saw him only hit two more home runs in the season's second half. I hope to see Gordon ditch the fly ball approach he took after the Royals changed hitting coaches. And if you draft him, you hope so too. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Alex Gordon has consistently positive fantasy value. With Alex Gordon, one might say that even when he is overrated, he is still underrated. After being one of the better overall real-baseball outfielders in baseball since 2011, prominent columnists felt it important to write that Gordon was not worthy of being the 2014 American League MVP because of all the "Alex Gordon for MVP" hype generated by, uh, pretty much no one. Sure, a lot of that had to do with backlash against stuff like wins above replacement and fielding metrics, but it still highlights just how unappreciated Gordon has been. In fantasy baseball, many of the things that he does in real baseball don't count -- such as excellent defense and smart base running. As a hitter, nothing really stands out. He usually has an average strikeout rate, and he is basically a bit above-average when it comes to walks and power. He is not going to carry any one category. The high batting averages on balls in play of 2011 and 2012 are gone, but he's still managed to be very productive on offense. Gordon's conditioning is legendary (he is rumored to have last have had pizza while he attended high school) and he has played 151 or more games each of the last four seasons. His likely 2015 line is not all that different from his 2008 sophomore season. What is different is that in 2008 that sort of line was considered okay-ish from a third baseman, and these days it is pretty good for a left fielder. The run environment matters in fantasy leagues. That is not to say Gordon did not produce fantasy value, just that although he was an elite player in real baseball starting in 2011, he was not quite on that level in fantasy. In 2015, Gordon was just getting rolling at the plate when a groin injury cost him more than a third of the season. When he came back, Gordon never really got back into a rhythm. Gordon has been extremely durable since moving to the outfield in prior to 2011, and his dedication to conditioning is well-known, so if you were going to bet on a player coming back to form, it would be Gordon. Still, hard work, diet, and exercise can only do so much against Father Time. Gordon will be 32 at the beginning of the season, did not hit a triple in 2015 (and hit only one in 2014), and only stole two bases after reaching double digits for several seasons in a row before that. That is not to say that Gordon is not still a valuable fantasy outfielder. He is probably a fairly durable outfielder that will give decent production this season. Hopefully the Royals will figure that out and, for their own good (as well as the counting stats fantasy owners crave) put him back in the top half of the order this season -- even after the injury, having him near the bottom of the order behind out machines like Alcides Escobar and Salvador Perez was ridiculous. It was his .220 AVG that brought down his overall value, however. Gordon's low production may have been linked to a fractured wrist, but it's tough to blame the injury for all of his struggles. The jump happened because he was not able to make contact with pitches out of the strike zone (50%) compared to his career average (59%). With his recent struggles, his fantasy value is tough to pin down. For 2017, Gordon’s value will be determined by how much he can bring his strikeout rate down. He may be a decent late-round sleeper option. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>A wrist injury and a jump in his strikeout rate ruined Gordon's 2016 season.
