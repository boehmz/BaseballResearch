                <tr id="so6" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>One of baseball's more pleasant surprises in 2013, Tanner Roark carried some fantasy owners down the stretch, posting a 1.74 ERA and 3-1 record in five September starts. Combining Roark's work in the rotation and out of the pen resulted in a 3.34 SIERA during his rookie year. Command is where he made his money, only walking 5% of the batters he faced. This isn't a new occurrence, either. He hasn't posted a double-digit walk rate since 2008 when he was in rookie ball with the Rangers. Unfortunately for his fantasy upside, he doesn't strike out too many guys either. He has decent velocity, sitting in the low 90's but he throws a heavy fastball to contact rather than blowing guys away. He has a good shot at putting up 50%+ ground-ball numbers, though, which helps keep the ball in the park and minimizes extra base hits against. Even if we assume he has the potential to be a solid WHIP contributor for your fantasy team, his real value hinges on whether or not he wins a spot in the Nationals rotation. Headed into spring training, he's probably the favorite for the final open spot, but with Stephen Strasburg, Jordan Zimmermann, Gio Gonzalez, and Doug Fister already locking up starting gigs, Roark will have to fend off all comers if he wants to stay out of the bullpen. Should he start, he's a nice gamble in the late rounds -- his strikeouts (or lack thereof) cap his ceiling, but he could be a nice back-of-the-rotation starter in deeper mixed leagues. (<a href="http://www.twitter.com/soxczar">Colin Zarzycki</a>)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Roark burst onto the scene in 2013 with a 1.53 ERA after a mid-season callup by Washington. A groundballer who is stingy with the walks but doesn't blow hitters away, his value hinges on whether or not he can lock down a rotation spot on a crowded Nationals pitching staff.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so7" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Roark doesn't display any great single talent, but has no horrible flaws either. The 28-year-old doesn't strike out a bunch of hitters, as he has a fairly pedestrian 6.4 strikeout per nine rate in the majors. If one aspect should get more love than others, that would be his walk rate, which sits under two per nine. Both of these can be combined (and manipulated a bit) to form a 12.4% strikeout minus walk rate, which is slightly above average for a major league pitcher. Pitchers with a lower strikeout rate than Roark last year included Chris Archer, Dallas Keuchel, Jered Weaver, Yordano Yentura, and teammate Doug Fister. Not great pitchers, but still useful.  His 43% ground-ball rate is near the league average, so it isn't a problem. Remember, in most cases, average is fine. He could see his ERA jump because it was nearly a run less than his ERA estimators. The ERA has been suppressed because of a low batting average on balls in play (.265) and high strand rate (79%, 70% is average). With another full season of pitching, he should put up similar numbers to his 2014 values with maybe fewer wins and a higher ERA. He is not a pitcher who will single-handedly win a league, but he will be a nice complementary piece in most leagues. (Jeff Zimmerman)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Tanner Roark has some fantasy values, as his recent peripherals are on par with several other well-regarded pitchers. Don't be afraid to own him if he is valued correctly.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>
                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Roark had two different month-long stints as a starter, posting a 4.82 ERA in 65 innings, while surrendering a .350 weighted on base average. His 2014 season was a lovely surprise for the Nationals, and he’s going to have to prove himself again before anyone can start to expect that kind of output on an annual basis. Is he capable of repeating that surprise season? He uses his sinker over half of the time and tries to generate a plethora of ground balls and give his infielders chances to make plays. He doesn’t get many whiffs with the sinker, and only pitched to a 41.4% ground ball rate in 2014. His changeup is the pitch that he gets the most whiffs with, but he’ll need to use it a little more than the 10% he did in 2014 if he wants to up his strikeouts. Being able to focus on starting should be a big help for Roark. I’m not convinced that his 2014 was for real, his sinker doesn’t generate a ton of grounders, and he doesn’t get enough strikeouts. Don’t expect a 2.85 ERA again -- Steamer agrees, projecting him with 140 or so innings and an ERA north of four. I’m in accord with Steamer. Don’t overpay for Roark, let someone else be blinded by his 2014. (Darren Scheinbein)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>After bouncing between the bullpen and the rotation in 2015, Roark is penciled in as the fourth starter in Washington. In 31 2014 starts, Roark gave you a 2.85 ERA and 15 wins. He wasn’t much for strikeouts, with a strikeout rate of 17.3%, but he kept the ball on the ground and gave you four outs shy of 200 innings. If he can put up 180 innings focusing on starting this year, he’s worth looking in to.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

Roark is one of the other guys. Taken in the 25th round, the command-and-control righty was traded for the shambling corpse of Cristian Guzman in 2010. Guzman went on to hit .152/.204/.174 in 50 plate appearances with the Rangers, then he retired. Roark wasn't even the "best" prospect in the trade - some sources thought Ryan Tatusko had a better chance of reaching the majors. As we know, Roark did eventually reach the bigs. After posting 7.3 RA-9 WAR (that's ERA-based WAR) through his first 252.1 innings, the Nationals did the sensible thing and signed Max Scherzer. The mega-deal bumped Roark to the bullpen. Incidentally, 2015 was something of a lost season for the righty. He recovered last year with a 210 inning, 2.83 ERA performance (6.2 RA-9 WAR). Roark consistently overperforms his peripherals via a high soft-contact rate. He's kind of like a poor man's Kyle Hendricks. Do note, the contact management pitching profile can be very volatile. See Keuchel, Dallas. (Brad Johnson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Roark was a nobody until his age-26 season when the Nationals were forced to admit that his command and control might play.
