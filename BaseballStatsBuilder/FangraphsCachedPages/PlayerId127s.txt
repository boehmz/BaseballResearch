                <tr id="so8" style="background-color: #FFF; display:none;"><td colspan="5" padding:3px; text-align:justify;"><div class="m_stories" style="font-size:10pt"><center><table><tr><td style="width:615px; padding-right:7px; text-align:justify;" valign="top"><b>Profile: </b>Among starters with at least 40 innings pitched, only seven other pitchers had a lower weighted on base average when facing left-handed hitting than Rodon. This speaks to the type of floor we're seeing with the 23-year-old lefty, as his dominating slider (.247 weighted on base average against in 2015) and electric mid 90s fastball conspire to overwhelm same-handed batters. The problem, of course, is the righties, who managed a rough .350 wOBA against him, as he lacks a third pitch and showed an inability to consistently get ahead of hitters with his fastball during the 2015 season. That's to be expected out of a young pitcher, even one with a golden arm, and Rodon will almost certainly have a chance to work on those issues in full-time major league work in 2016. The other good news is that he showed improved command during the second half of last season, cutting his walk rate significantly from the first half (13.9% vs. 9.7%), and he also showed a willingness to fully commit to throwing his changeup after June. Make no mistake: Rodon is an unfinished product, but he has the tools and talent to be a number two starter in the big leagues in the nearish future. (Owen Watson)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Sheesh, that slider. At only 22 years old, Rodon showed about what we would expect in 2015 out of such a young prospect making it to the big leagues: electric stuff, erratic control, and an overall lack of consistency. Heading into 2016, we can expect some growing pains to continue: he still needs to find a viable third pitch to help eliminate his platoon problems, and his control and command need a lot of improvement. Still, there's great upside here (especially for keeper leagues), the kind of upside that puts you at the top of a rotation.</td></tr></table></center></div></td></tr>

In '16, Rodon made an adjustment that led to a great finish -- a 3.45 ERA, totally supported this time by a jump in strikeouts after deciding to throw more four-seamers and changeups instead of so many sinkers. If you believe the last 73 innings of last year, in which he struck out more than a quarter of the batters he saw and walked fewer than average, he can be an ace this coming season. The lefty always had this in him. The only pause comes from the fact that we've seen him do this sort of thing twice now, and his numbers on both full seasons have both been fairly mediocre. Pay for the projections, for sure. But if he can control his four-seamer better, and now trusts his changeup more, Rodon is going to be better than he was before. That would make him a fantasy number two in any league, although that hopeful outcome is be based on adjustments he's already made. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>With a new spot on the rubber and a switch to four-seamers, Rodon's walk rate has improved in leaps and bounds.
