Regardless, he began the season well at Triple-A Toledo, producing a roughly 30% strikeout rate over his first seven starts and 38.0 innings. That success didn't translate to the majors, however: he failed to strike out even 13% of hitters he faced there over 40.1 innings, a number of those appearance in relief. Neither line is probably indicative of Farmer's future, though. He possess both above-average fastball velocity and an above-average changeup -- and a build that suggests durability. The profile would appear to suggest that a career as a mid-rotation starter is still possible, although it'll require some refinement in his command, it seems, to contend with major league batters. He's almost certain to get more chances with the parent club this season. (Carson Cistulli)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>There were few positives for Farmer during his 40-inning stay with the parent club in 2015. That's too bad, because he has at least a major league-average slider to go with his plus change. In fact, if you take out the sidearmers, Farmer's change has top-25 drop in the major leagues (minimum 100 thrown). The problem might be that Farmer's fastball is straight. Average movement on that pitch, paired with only slightly above-average velocity, has given him average whiffs but also a line drive and home run problem. That and a few hanging sliders have led to a move to the pen. If he prepares for a life in relief and gets more than a tick or two in velocity, then maybe that fastball gets a little less straight and his secondary pitches play up. Fewer sliders would also mean fewer homers. As a starter, a three-pitch arsenal in an okay park might produce league average numbers, which is interesting in an AL-Only league. As a swing man, he's not so useful to anyone. Stash the name and wait for a role change that will allow him to showcase that changeup. (Eno Sarris)<br /><br /><b>The Quick Opinion: </b>Right now, Farmer is in between the bullpen and the starting rotation.
